Backbone probability of planar Brownian motion

Gefei Cai  Zhuoyan Xie{}^{*} Peking University.
Abstract

Motivated by critical planar percolation, we investigate a “backbone” event of planar Brownian motion, i.e. the existence of two disjoint subpaths on the Brownian trajectory connecting the \varepsilon-neighborhood of the starting point to a macroscopic distance. We show that the probability of this event is (\log|\log\varepsilon|)^{-1} up to a multiplicative constant.

1 Introduction

1.1 Overview and the main result

There is a strong relation between the planar Brownian motion and critical percolation. In particular, for n\geq 1, the Brownian intersection exponent \zeta_{n}=\frac{4n^{2}-1}{12} [LSW01a, LSW01b, LSW02] is the same as the alternating 2n-arm exponent of critical percolation [SW01]. This can be well understood in the view of conformal restriction [LSW03]: both of the hulls of Brownian motion and percolation cluster satisfy the restriction property, and therefore, their outer boundaries can both be described by Schramm-Loewner evolution (SLE) with parameter \kappa=\frac{8}{3}.

Note that in the critical percolation, one can also define the monochromatic arm exponents corresponding to that there exist n disjoint arms of the same color joining two boundaries of an annulus. Based on the relation between Brownian motion and critical percolation, it is natural to explore the analog of such monochromatic arm exponents in the context of Brownian motion. Recently, [NQSZ24] derives the exact value of percolation monochromatic 2-arm exponent, namely the backbone exponent. In this paper, we investigate its Brownian counterpart, and find that such Brownian backbone probability indeed has an iterated logarithmic decay (so the “Brownian backbone exponent” is equal to 0). This also shows a big difference between Brownian motion and critical percolation, although their outer boundaries (or hulls) are the same.

To be precise, let (B_{t})_{t\geq 0} be a planar Brownian motion starting from 0. Denote \mathbbm{S}^{1}:=\{z\in\mathbbm{C}:|z|=1\} and \mathbbm{D}:=\{z\in\mathbbm{C}:|z|<1\} to be the unit circle and the unit disk, respectively. Let \tau_{\mathbbm{D}} be the first hitting time of \mathbbm{S}^{1} for (B_{t})_{t\geq 0}. For each \varepsilon\in(0,\frac{1}{2}), consider the event

\mathsf{Bac}_{\varepsilon}=\{\exists\ \text{two disjoint subpaths on the % trajectory }B[0,\tau_{\mathbbm{D}}]\text{ joining }\varepsilon\mathbbm{S}^{1}% \text{ and }\frac{1}{2}\mathbbm{S}^{1}\}. (1.1)

Namely, \mathsf{Bac}_{\varepsilon} happens if there are two continuous curves \gamma^{1},\gamma^{2}:[0,1]\to\mathbbm{C} such that \gamma^{1}[0,1]\cap\gamma^{2}[0,1]=\emptyset, \gamma^{i}[0,1]\subset B[0,\tau_{\mathbbm{D}}], \gamma^{i}(0)\in\varepsilon\mathbbm{S}^{1}, and \gamma^{i}(1)\in\frac{1}{2}\mathbbm{S}^{1} for i=1,2. Using terminology from percolation, we call \mathsf{Bac}_{\varepsilon} the backbone event for the Brownian trajectory B[0,\tau_{\mathbbm{D}}].

The main result in this paper is the following iterated logarithmic decay for the probability \mathbbm{P}[\mathsf{Bac}_{\varepsilon}].

Theorem 1.1.

There exist constants C_{1},C_{2}>0 such that for any \varepsilon\in(0,\frac{1}{10}),

\frac{C_{1}}{\log|\log\varepsilon|}\leq\mathbbm{P}[\mathsf{Bac}_{\varepsilon}]% \leq\frac{C_{2}}{\log|\log\varepsilon|}. (1.2)
Remark 1.2.

The same result also holds when \frac{1}{2}\mathbbm{S}^{1} in (1.1) is changed to any fixed r\mathbbm{S}^{1} with r\in(0,1) (and for sufficiently small \varepsilon), except that the corresponding constants in (1.2) will depend on r.

Note that the backbone event \mathsf{Bac}_{\varepsilon} is closely related to the spatial distribution of the cut points of B[0,\tau_{\mathbbm{D}}]. Indeed, our proof of Theorem 1.1 is based on our recent paper with Fu and Sun [CFSX25], from which we can define a layer structure for the cut points of B[0,\tau_{\mathbbm{D}}] and then solve it explicitly; see Section 3. We mention that [CFSX25] heavily relies on the connection between planar Brownian motion and \operatorname{SLE}_{8/3}, and especially their coupling with Liouville quantum gravity (LQG). It would be quite interesting to see if there is a derivation of Theorem 1.1 without relying on LQG.

Theorem 1.1 is related to a certain kind of special points of Brownian motion. Namely, let

\mathcal{B}:=\{B_{s}:s\in[0,\tau_{\mathbbm{D}})\text{ and there exists }% \varepsilon>0\text{ such that }(B_{s+u})_{0\leq u\leq\varepsilon}\text{ does % not have a cut point}\}.

Then Theorem 1.1 indicates that \mathcal{B} is non-empty and has Hausdorff dimension 2. Furthermore, it also suggests that the Hausdorff measure of \mathcal{B} with the gauge r\mapsto r^{2}\log\frac{1}{r}\log\log\frac{1}{r}\log\log\log\frac{1}{r} would exist and be non-trivial (since the Hausdorff gauge of B[0,\tau_{\mathbbm{D}}] itself is r\mapsto r^{2}\log\frac{1}{r}\log\log\log\frac{1}{r}, see e.g. [LG85]). In particular, due to that planar Brownian motion a.s. has no double cut points [BL90], we know that \mathcal{B} contains the set of double points of (B_{t})_{0\leq t\leq\tau_{\mathbbm{D}}}, which has the Hausdorff gauge r\mapsto r^{2}(\log\frac{1}{r}\log\log\log\frac{1}{r})^{2} [LG87].

Organization of the paper. In Section 2, we review the explicit relation between the laws of conformal radii and moduli for \operatorname{SLE}_{8/3}-type loops. Such relation was implicitly established in [ARS25b], and we will provide its proof in Appendix A for completeness. Basic notations. For two compact sets A,B\subset\mathbbm{C}, let \mathrm{dist}(A,B):=\inf\{|a-b|:a\in A,b\in B\}. For a simple loop \ell\subset\mathbbm{C}, let D(\ell) be the bounded connected component of \mathbbm{C}\backslash\ell. For a simple loop \ell\subset\mathbbm{C} with 0\in D(\ell), we denote the conformal radius of D(\ell) seen from 0 by \mathrm{CR}(\ell,0), i.e. if f:\mathbbm{D}\to D(\ell) is a conformal map that fixes the origin, then \mathrm{CR}(\ell,0)=|f^{\prime}(0)|.

For 0<r<1, let \mathbbm{A}_{r} be the standard annulus \{z\in\mathbbm{C}:r<|z|<1\}. For each annular domain A\subset\mathbbm{C}, there exists a unique \tau>0 such that A and \mathbbm{A}_{e^{-2\pi\tau}} are conformally equivalent. We call \tau the modulus of A and write \mathrm{Mod}(A):=\tau. For two simple loops \eta_{1},\eta_{2} such that \overline{D(\eta_{1})}\subset D(\eta_{2}), we also write the modulus of the annular domain D(\eta_{2})\setminus\overline{D(\eta_{1})} as \mathrm{Mod}(\eta_{1},\eta_{2}) for simplicity.

We will frequently deal with elementary functions such as \sqrt{x},\sinh(\sqrt{x}),\cosh(\sqrt{x}), and \tanh(\sqrt{x}). In the following, we view them as functions defined on \mathbbm{R} by taking i\sqrt{|x|},i\sin(\sqrt{|x|}),\cos(\sqrt{|x|}), and i\tan(\sqrt{|x|}) for x<0, respectively.

2 Conformal radii of \operatorname{SLE}_{8/3}-type loops

The \operatorname{SLE}_{8/3} loop measure on \mathbbm{C} is a canonical infinite measure on simple loops characterized by conformal restriction [Wer08], which can be obtained by taking outer boundaries from the Brownian loop measure on \mathbbm{C}. According to [GM19, AHS23], the \operatorname{SLE}_{8/3} loop measure on \mathbbm{C} also describes the welding interface when conformally welding two independent Brownian disks into a Brownian sphere.

Let \operatorname{SLE}^{\mathrm{loop}}_{8/3,\mathbbm{D}} be the \operatorname{SLE}_{8/3} loop measure on \mathbbm{C} restricted to the loops that are contained in \mathbbm{D} and surround 0. By [ARS25b], \operatorname{SLE}_{8/3,\mathbbm{D}}^{\mathrm{loop}} is the welding interface when comformally welding a Brownian disk and an independent Brownian annulus into a larger Brownian disk. This, combined with integrability of Liouville conformal field theory on the annulus by [Wu22], gives the following

Theorem 2.1 ([ARS25b]).

Let \mu_{\mathbbm{D}} be a measure on simple loops contained in \mathbbm{D} and surrounding 0, such that \frac{d\mu_{\mathbbm{D}}}{d\operatorname{SLE}^{\mathrm{loop}}_{8/3,\mathbbm{D}% }}(\eta)=f(\mathrm{Mod}(\eta,\mathbbm{S}^{1})) for some positive continuous function f:\mathbbm{R}_{+}\to\mathbbm{R}_{+}. Then there is a constant C>0 such that for each \lambda\geq 0,

\int\mathrm{CR}(\eta,0)^{\lambda}\mu_{\mathbbm{D}}(d\eta)=C\frac{\sqrt{12% \lambda-1}}{\sinh\left(\frac{\pi}{3}\sqrt{12\lambda-1}\right)}\int_{0}^{\infty% }e^{-(2\lambda-\frac{1}{6})\pi\tau}\eta(2i\tau)f(\tau)d\tau.

Here \eta(2i\tau)=e^{-\frac{\pi}{6}\tau}\prod_{n=1}^{\infty}(1-e^{-4\pi n\tau}) is the Dedekind eta function. When \lambda\in(0,\frac{1}{12}), the right side is defined by analytic continuation; see the end of Section 1.

The proof of Theorem 2.1 is implicit in [ARS25b], and we will provide a proof in Appendix A for completeness. In the following, we turn to the proof of Theorem 1.1.

3 The exact laws of the conformal radii

For the Brownian trajectory B[0,\tau_{\mathbbm{D}}], we say t\in(0,\tau_{\mathbbm{D}}) is a cut time if B[0,t)\cap B(t,\tau_{\mathbbm{D}}]=\emptyset, and the corresponding B_{t} is called a cut point. Note that for any \varepsilon>0, B[0,\tau_{\mathbbm{D}}] has infinitely many cut points contained in \varepsilon\mathbbm{D} [Bur89, Theorem 2.2].

Definition 3.1.

Let (t_{i})_{i\geq 1} be the collection of cut time t’s of B[0,\tau_{\mathbbm{D}}] such that the outer boundary of B[0,t] is a simple loop, ranked in decreasing order. Note that a.s. \lim_{i\to\infty}t_{i}=0. For each i\geq 1, define s_{i} to be the last cut time before t_{i} (thus t_{i}>s_{i}>t_{i+1}). Let \ell_{i} and \widetilde{\ell}_{i} be the outer boundary of B[0,t_{i}] and the boundary of the connected component of \mathbbm{D}\setminus B[s_{i},t_{i}] containing the origin, respectively.

Let f be the conformal map from D(\ell_{1}) to \mathbbm{D} with f(0)=0, f^{\prime}(0)>0 and let \rho=f(\widetilde{\ell}_{1}). Note that \mathrm{CR}(\rho,0)=\frac{\mathrm{CR}(\widetilde{\ell}_{1},0)}{\mathrm{CR}(% \ell_{1},0)}. The main result of this section is the following exact law of \mathrm{CR}(\rho,0), which is crucial to the final proof of Theorem 1.1 in Section 4.

Proposition 3.2.

For \lambda\geq 0, we have

\mathbbm{E}[\mathrm{CR}(\rho,0)^{\lambda}]=\frac{\sinh(\frac{\pi}{2}\sqrt{12% \lambda-1})}{\sqrt{12\lambda-1}}-\frac{2\sqrt{3}\sinh(\frac{\pi}{3}\sqrt{12% \lambda-1})}{\sqrt{12\lambda-1}\log\left(\frac{(2+\sqrt{3})^{2}+\tanh^{2}\left% (\frac{\pi}{12}\sqrt{12\lambda-1}\right)}{1+(2+\sqrt{3})^{2}\tanh^{2}\left(% \frac{\pi}{12}\sqrt{12\lambda-1}\right)}\right)}. (3.1)

When \lambda\in[0,\frac{1}{12}), the right side of (3.1) is defined by analytic continuation; see the end of Section 1.

The proof of Proposition 3.2 relies on the exact solvability of \mathrm{CR}(\ell_{i},0) for each i\geq 1 in Definition 3.1. The following explicit law of \mathrm{CR}(\ell_{1},0) is originally derived by [Qia21, Theorem 1.5] using the radial hypergeometric SLE; see [CFSX25, Remark 4.9] for another derivation using LQG.

Lemma 3.3.

For \lambda\geq 0, we have

\mathbbm{E}[\mathrm{CR}(\ell_{1},0)^{\lambda}]=\frac{\sqrt{12\lambda-1}}{\sinh% (\frac{\pi}{2}\sqrt{12\lambda-1})}. (3.2)

When \lambda\in[0,\frac{1}{12}), the right side of (3.2) is defined by analytic continuation. In particular, \mathbbm{P}[\mathrm{CR}(\ell_{1},0)<\varepsilon]=\frac{16}{3\pi}\varepsilon^{% \frac{1}{4}}(1+o(1)) as \varepsilon\downarrow 0.

Let \nu_{\mathbbm{D}} be the law of the loop chosen from the counting measure over \{\ell_{i}\}_{i\geq 1}; namely, \nu_{\mathbbm{D}} is such that \int F(\eta)\nu_{\mathbbm{D}}(d\eta)=\mathbbm{P}\left[\sum_{i\geq 1}F(\ell_{i}% )\right] for any bounded measurable function F. The following result, based on [CFSX25] and Theorem 2.1, gives the explicit law of the conformal radius under \nu_{\mathbbm{D}}.

Lemma 3.4.

There is a constant C>0 such that for \lambda\geq 0, we have

\int\mathrm{CR}(\eta,0)^{\lambda}\nu_{\mathbbm{D}}(d\eta)=C\frac{\sqrt{12% \lambda-1}}{\sinh(\frac{\pi}{3}\pi\sqrt{12\lambda-1})}\log\left(\frac{(2+\sqrt% {3})^{2}+\tanh^{2}\left(\frac{\pi}{12}\sqrt{12\lambda-1}\right)}{1+(2+\sqrt{3}% )^{2}\tanh^{2}\left(\frac{\pi}{12}\sqrt{12\lambda-1}\right)}\right). (3.3)

When \lambda\in(0,\frac{1}{12}), the right side of (3.3) is defined via analytic continuation as before.

Proof.

By combining [CFSX25, Lemma 5.3] with [CFSX25, Theorem 1.3], we have \frac{d\nu_{\mathbbm{D}}}{d\operatorname{SLE}^{\mathrm{loop}}_{8/3,\mathbbm{D}% }}(\eta)=\frac{C_{1}}{\mathrm{Mod}(\eta,\mathbbm{S}^{1})} for some constant C_{1}>0. Then by Theorem 2.1, we find

\int\mathrm{CR}(\eta,0)^{\lambda}\nu_{\mathbbm{D}}(d\eta)=C\frac{\sqrt{12% \lambda-1}}{\sinh(\frac{\pi}{3}\sqrt{12\lambda-1})}\int_{0}^{\infty}e^{-(2% \lambda-\frac{1}{6})\pi\tau}\frac{\eta(2i\tau)}{\tau}d\tau. (3.4)

for some constant C>0. Note that for a>-\frac{\pi}{6}, we have \int_{0}^{\infty}e^{-a\tau}\eta(2i\tau)d\tau=\sqrt{\frac{\pi}{2a}}\frac{\sinh% \left(\sqrt{\frac{2}{3}\pi a}\right)}{\cosh\left(\sqrt{\frac{3}{2}\pi a}\right)} (see e.g. [ARS25b, Equation (A.4)]) and

\frac{d}{da}\left[-\log\left(\frac{(2+\sqrt{3})^{2}+\tanh^{2}\left(\frac{1}{2}% \sqrt{\frac{\pi}{6}a}\right)}{(2-\sqrt{3})^{2}+\tanh^{2}\left(\frac{1}{2}\sqrt% {\frac{\pi}{6}a}\right)}\right)\right]=\sqrt{\frac{\pi}{2a}}\frac{\sinh\left(% \sqrt{\frac{2}{3}\pi a}\right)}{\cosh\left(\sqrt{\frac{3}{2}\pi a}\right)}.

Therefore, we obtain

\int_{0}^{\infty}e^{-a\tau}\frac{\eta(2i\tau)}{\tau}d\tau=\log\left(\frac{(2+% \sqrt{3})^{2}+\tanh^{2}\left(\frac{1}{2}\sqrt{\frac{\pi}{6}a}\right)}{1+(2+% \sqrt{3})^{2}\tanh^{2}\left(\frac{1}{2}\sqrt{\frac{\pi}{6}a}\right)}\right).

for a>-\frac{\pi}{6}. Combined with the right side of (3.4), we conclude. ∎

To obtain Proposition 3.2 from Lemmas 3.3 and 3.4, we also need the following i.i.d. structure of \{\ell_{i}\}_{i\geq 1}, which is a radial variant of the Brownian beads decomposition established in [Vir03].

Lemma 3.5.

We have:

  1. (i)

    Let g:D(\ell_{1})\to\mathbbm{D} be the conformal map such that g(0)=0 and g(B_{t_{1}})=1. Then g(\widetilde{\ell}_{1}) is independent of \ell_{1}. In particular, \mathrm{CR}(\rho,0) is independent of \ell_{1}.

  2. (ii)

    Let \widetilde{g}:D(\widetilde{\ell}_{1})\to\mathbbm{D} be the conformal map such that \widetilde{g}(0)=0 and \widetilde{g}(B_{s_{1}})=1. Then (\widetilde{g}(B_{t}))_{t\in[0,s_{1}]} is independent of (B_{t})_{t\in[s_{1},\tau_{\mathbbm{D}}]}, and has the same law (up to a time change) as (B_{t})_{t\in[0,\tau_{\mathbbm{D}}]} conditioned on B_{\tau_{\mathbbm{D}}}=1.

As a corollary, if we set \widetilde{\ell}_{0}=\mathbbm{S}^{1}, then \left(\frac{\mathrm{CR}(\widetilde{\ell}_{n},0)}{\mathrm{CR}(\ell_{n},0)}% \right)_{n\geq 1} and \left(\frac{\mathrm{CR}(\ell_{n+1},0)}{\mathrm{CR}(\widetilde{\ell}_{n},0)}% \right)_{n\geq 0} are two i.i.d. r.v. sequences, and mutually independent of each other.

Proof.

We first prove (ii). Due to rotational invariance, it suffices to consider the case when conditioning on B_{\tau_{\mathbbm{D}}}=1. Let (Y_{t})_{t\geq 0} be the time reversal of (B_{t})_{t\in[0,\tau_{\mathbbm{D}}]} conditioned on B_{\tau_{\mathbbm{D}}}=1. Note that Y_{t} is indeed a Brownian excursion in \mathbbm{D} from 1 to 0. Let (\mathcal{F}_{t})_{t\geq 0} be the natural filtration of (Y_{t})_{t\geq 0}, and (\mathcal{G}_{t})_{t\geq 0} be the cut time filtration defined in [Vir03, Section 3] (i.e. \mathcal{G}_{t} is the \sigma-field generated by \mathcal{F}_{t} and the set of all cut times of (Y_{t})_{t\geq 0} before time t). By [Vir03, Proposition 14], if \tau is a (\mathcal{G}_{t})_{t\geq 0}-stopping time such that \tau is also a cut time of (Y_{t})_{t\geq 0}, then the conditional law of (Y_{\tau+u})_{u\geq 0} given \mathcal{G}_{\tau} is a Brownian excursion from Y_{\tau} to 0 in the connected component of \mathbbm{D}\backslash Y[0,\tau] containing 0. Now we consider s_{1}^{\prime}:=\tau_{\mathbbm{D}}-s_{1}. Note that s_{1}^{\prime} is the first cut time of (Y_{t})_{t\geq 0} such that Y[0,s^{\prime}_{1}] disconnects the origin from \mathbbm{S}^{1}, thus is a (\mathcal{G}_{t})_{t\geq 0}-stopping time. Therefore, the conditional law of (\widetilde{g}(Y_{s_{1}^{\prime}+u}))_{u\geq 0} given \mathcal{G}_{s_{1}^{\prime}} is an independent Brownian excursion (up to a time change) in \mathbbm{D} from 1 to 0. By taking the time reversal of (Y_{t})_{t\geq 0}, we obtain the result.

Then we prove (i). Let A\subset\overline{\mathbbm{D}} be relatively compact such that \mathbbm{D}\backslash A is simply connected and contains 0. Note that the law of (B_{t})_{t\in[0,\tau_{\mathbbm{D}}]} conditioned on the event B[0,\tau_{\mathbbm{D}}]\cap A=\emptyset is the same as the Brownian motion (up to a time change) in \mathbbm{D}\backslash A starting from 0 and conditioned on exiting \mathbbm{D}\backslash A at \mathbbm{S}^{1}\setminus A. Combined with the conformal invariance of the Brownian motion, it then implies that g(\widetilde{\ell}_{1}) is independent of the event B[0,\tau_{\mathbbm{D}}]\cap A=\emptyset. By varying A, we conclude that g(\widetilde{\ell}_{1}) is independent of the outer boundary of B[0,\tau_{\mathbbm{D}}], and hence \ell_{1}. ∎

Now Proposition 3.2 follows by combining Lemma 3.5 and Lemmas 3.3 and 3.4 above.

Proof of Proposition 3.2.

By Lemma 3.5 and the definition of \nu_{\mathbbm{D}}, we have

\displaystyle\int\mathrm{CR}(\eta,0)^{\lambda}\nu_{\mathbbm{D}}(d\eta) \displaystyle=\sum\limits_{n\geq 1}\mathbbm{E}[\mathrm{CR}(\ell_{n},0)^{% \lambda}]=\sum\limits_{n\geq 1}\mathbbm{E}\left[\left(\prod\limits_{k=2}^{n}% \frac{\mathrm{CR}(\ell_{k},0)}{\mathrm{CR}(\widetilde{\ell}_{k-1},0)}\cdot% \frac{\mathrm{CR}(\widetilde{\ell}_{k-1},0)}{\mathrm{CR}(\ell_{k-1},0)}\right)% ^{\lambda}\mathrm{CR}(\ell_{1},0)^{\lambda}\right]
\displaystyle=\sum\limits_{n\geq 1}\mathbbm{E}[\mathrm{CR}(\ell_{1},0)^{% \lambda}]^{n}\mathbbm{E}[\mathrm{CR}(\rho,0)^{\lambda}]^{n-1}=\frac{\mathbbm{E% }[\mathrm{CR}(\ell_{1},0)^{\lambda}]}{1-\mathbbm{E}[\mathrm{CR}(\ell_{1},0)^{% \lambda}]\mathbbm{E}[\mathrm{CR}(\rho,0)^{\lambda}]}.

Thus, combined with Lemmas 3.3 and 3.4, we obtain

\displaystyle\mathbbm{E}[\mathrm{CR}(\rho,0)^{\lambda}] \displaystyle=\frac{1}{\mathbbm{E}[\mathrm{CR}(\ell_{1},0)^{\lambda}]}-\frac{1% }{\nu_{\mathbbm{D}}[\mathrm{CR}(\eta,0)^{\lambda}]}
\displaystyle=\frac{\sinh(\frac{\pi}{2}\sqrt{12\lambda-1})}{\sqrt{12\lambda-1}% }-\frac{\sinh(\frac{\pi}{3}\sqrt{12\lambda-1})}{C\sqrt{12\lambda-1}\log\left(% \frac{(2+\sqrt{3})^{2}+\tanh^{2}\left(\frac{\pi}{12}\sqrt{12\lambda-1}\right)}% {1+(2+\sqrt{3})^{2}\tanh^{2}\left(\frac{\pi}{12}\sqrt{12\lambda-1}\right)}% \right)}. (3.5)

Here the constant C is the same as the one in Lemma 3.4. Now, note that (3.5) becomes \frac{e^{\pi\sqrt{3\lambda}}}{4\sqrt{3\lambda}}(1-\frac{1}{2\sqrt{3}C})(1+o(1)) as \lambda\to\infty. Since we must have \mathbbm{E}[\mathrm{CR}(\rho,0)^{\lambda}]\to 0 as \lambda\to\infty, we find C=\frac{\sqrt{3}}{6}. Taking this into (3.5), we conclude. ∎

As a corollary of Proposition 3.2, by the following special case of Tauberian theorem, we can derive the tail probabilities of \mathrm{CR}(\rho,0) and \mathrm{CR}(\widetilde{\ell}_{1},0), which will be used in Section 4.

Lemma 3.6.

Suppose \mathcal{X} is a positive random variable such that \mathbbm{E}[1-e^{-\lambda\mathcal{X}}]=\frac{C}{|\log\lambda|}(1+o(1)),\ % \lambda\downarrow 0 for some constant C>0. Then \mathbbm{P}[\mathcal{X}>t]=\frac{C}{\log t}(1+o(1)) as t\to\infty.

Proof.

Note that \mathbbm{E}[1-e^{-\lambda\mathcal{X}}]\geq(1-e^{-\lambda t})\mathbbm{P}[% \mathcal{X}>t] for t>0. Fix M>0 and take \lambda=\frac{M}{t}, by letting t\to\infty, then we find \varlimsup\limits_{t\to\infty}\log t\cdot\mathbbm{P}[\mathcal{X}>t]\leq C(1-e^% {-M})^{-1}. Since M>0 is arbitrary, we have \varlimsup\limits_{t\to\infty}\log t\cdot\mathbbm{P}[\mathcal{X}>t]\leq C. On the other hand, note that for t>1,

\log t\cdot\mathbbm{E}[1-e^{-\lambda\mathcal{X}}]=\log t\cdot\int_{0}^{\infty}% \lambda e^{-\lambda s}\mathbbm{P}[\mathcal{X}>s]ds\leq\log t\cdot(1-e^{-% \lambda t})+\log t\cdot e^{-\lambda t}\mathbbm{P}[\mathcal{X}>t] (3.6)

Choose \alpha>1. Taking \lambda=t^{-\alpha} and then t\to\infty in (3.6) yields \varliminf\limits_{t\to\infty}\log t\cdot\mathbbm{P}[\mathcal{X}>t]\geq\alpha^% {-1}C. The lemma then follows by taking \alpha\downarrow 1. ∎

Corollary 3.7.

We have \mathbbm{P}[\mathrm{CR}(\rho,0)<\varepsilon]=\frac{3}{\log|\log\varepsilon|}(1% +o(1)) and \mathbbm{P}[\mathrm{CR}(\widetilde{\ell}_{1},0)<\varepsilon]=\frac{3}{\log|% \log\varepsilon|}(1+o(1)) as \varepsilon\downarrow 0.

Proof.

By Proposition 3.2, when \lambda\in[0,\frac{1}{12}), we have

\mathbbm{E}[\mathrm{CR}(\widetilde{\ell}_{1},0)^{\lambda}]=\mathbbm{E}[\mathrm% {CR}(\rho,0)^{\lambda}]\mathbbm{E}[\mathrm{CR}(\ell_{1},0)^{\lambda}]=1-\frac{% 2\sqrt{3}\sin(\frac{\pi}{3}\sqrt{1-12\lambda})}{\sin(\frac{\pi}{2}\sqrt{1-12% \lambda})\log\left(\frac{(2+\sqrt{3})^{2}-\tan^{2}\left(\frac{\pi}{12}\sqrt{1-% 12\lambda}\right)}{1-(2+\sqrt{3})^{2}\tan^{2}\left(\frac{\pi}{12}\sqrt{1-12% \lambda}\right)}\right)}.

Hence \mathbbm{E}[\mathrm{CR}(\widetilde{\ell}_{1},0)^{\lambda}]=1-\frac{3}{|\log% \lambda|}(1+o(1)) as \lambda\downarrow 0, and we conclude by using Lemma 3.6. ∎

4 Proof of Theorem 1.1

In this section, we prove Theorem 1.1 based on the exact laws of the conformal radii in Section 3. Recall that \mathsf{Bac}_{\varepsilon} is the event that there exist two disjoint subpaths on B[0,\tau_{\mathbbm{D}}] from \varepsilon\mathbbm{S}^{1} to \frac{1}{2}\mathbbm{S}^{1}. The lower bound of \mathbbm{P}[\mathsf{Bac}_{\varepsilon}] in (1.2) then relies on the following continuum version of Menger’s theorem on the connectedness of a graph.

Theorem 4.1 ([Nö32, Zip33, Why48]).

Let n\geq 2. Suppose X is a connected, locally connected, and locally compact metric space such that X cannot be disconnected by removing any n-1 points of X. Then for any two points x,y\in X, there exist n arcs connecting x and y, which are pairwise disjoint except on x and y. Here an arc stands for a subset of X that is homeomorphic to [0,1].

Lemma 4.2.

\mathsf{Bac}_{\varepsilon} occurs if \ell_{1}\cap\mathbbm{A}_{1/2}\neq\emptyset and \widetilde{\ell}_{1}\cap\varepsilon\mathbbm{D}\neq\emptyset.

Proof.

Note that B[s_{1},t_{1}] forms a connected, locally connected, and locally compact metric space when equipped with the Euclidean metric on \mathbbm{C}. By Definition 3.1, B[s_{1},t_{1}] cannot be disconnected by removing any single point (otherwise it would contradict the definition of s_{1}). Furthermore, on the event \{\ell_{1}\cap\mathbbm{A}_{1/2}\neq\emptyset\}\cap\{\widetilde{\ell}_{1}\cap% \varepsilon\mathbbm{D}\neq\emptyset\}, there are s,t\in[s_{1},t_{1}] such that B_{s}\in\varepsilon\mathbbm{D} and B_{t}\in\mathbbm{A}_{1/2}. Thus by Theorem 4.1, there exist two arcs on B[s_{1},t_{1}] from B_{s} to B_{t}, staying disjoint apart from their endpoints. The result then follows by taking the sub-trajectories of these two arcs from their last hitting points of \varepsilon\mathbbm{S}^{1} to their first hitting points of \frac{1}{2}\mathbbm{S}^{1}, respectively. ∎

Proof of Theorem 1.1, the lower bound.

By Lemma 4.2, we have \mathbbm{P}[\mathsf{Bac}_{\varepsilon}]\geq\mathbbm{P}[\ell_{1}\cap\mathbbm{A}% _{1/2}\neq\emptyset,\ \widetilde{\ell}_{1}\cap\varepsilon\mathbbm{D}\neq\emptyset]. Note that \mathbbm{P}[\ell_{1}\cap\mathbbm{A}_{1/2}\neq\emptyset]=p_{0}>0, which does not depend on \varepsilon. On the other hand,

\displaystyle\mathbbm{P}[\widetilde{\ell}_{1}\cap\varepsilon\mathbbm{D}\neq% \emptyset|\ell_{1}\cap\mathbbm{A}_{1/2}\neq\emptyset] \displaystyle\geq\mathbbm{P}[\mathrm{CR}(\widetilde{\ell}_{1},0)<\varepsilon|% \ell_{1}\cap\mathbbm{A}_{1/2}\neq\emptyset]
\displaystyle\geq\mathbbm{P}[\mathrm{CR}(\rho,0)<\varepsilon|\ell_{1}\cap% \mathbbm{A}_{1/2}\neq\emptyset]

due to Schwartz’s lemma and that \mathrm{CR}(\rho,0)=\frac{\mathrm{CR}(\widetilde{\ell}_{1},0)}{\mathrm{CR}(% \ell_{1},0)}>\mathrm{CR}(\widetilde{\ell}_{1},0). By Lemma 3.5, \mathrm{CR}(\rho,0) is independent of \ell_{1}. Therefore, combined with Corollary 3.7, we have

\mathbbm{P}[\mathsf{Bac}_{\varepsilon}]\geq p_{0}\mathbbm{P}[\mathrm{CR}(\rho,% 0)<\varepsilon]\geq\frac{C_{1}}{\log|\log\varepsilon|}

for some positive constant C_{1}, as desired. ∎

Now we turn to the upper bound of \mathbbm{P}[\mathsf{Bac}_{\varepsilon}]. We first need the following basic fact.

Lemma 4.3.

Let \varepsilon\in(0,10^{-2}). Suppose \ell\subset\mathbbm{D} is a simple loop such that \mathrm{dist}(\ell,0)>5\sqrt{\varepsilon}, and let g:D(\ell)\to\mathbbm{D} be any conformal map such that g(0)=0. By applying Schwartz’s lemma for g^{-1}, we have g(\frac{1}{2}\mathbbm{D}\cap D(\ell))\supset\frac{1}{2}\mathbbm{D}, and \frac{1}{|g^{\prime}(0)|}=\mathrm{CR}(\ell,0)\geq\mathrm{dist}(\ell,0)>5\sqrt{\varepsilon}. Let \widetilde{g}(z):=\frac{|g^{\prime}(0)|}{5\sqrt{\varepsilon}}g^{-1}(5\sqrt{% \varepsilon}z); note that \widetilde{g}(0)=0 and |\widetilde{g}^{\prime}(0)|=1. By Koebe’s 1/4 theorem, we have \frac{1}{4}\mathbbm{D}\subset\widetilde{g}(\mathbbm{D}), which implies \frac{1}{4}\mathbbm{D}\subset\frac{|g^{\prime}(0)|}{5\sqrt{\varepsilon}}g^{-1}% (5\sqrt{\varepsilon}\mathbbm{D})\subset\frac{1}{5\varepsilon}g^{-1}(5\sqrt{% \varepsilon}\mathbbm{D}), as desired. ∎

The upper bound of \mathbbm{P}[\mathsf{Bac}_{\varepsilon}] then relies on the following iterative inequality.

Proposition 4.4.

Note that

\displaystyle\mathbbm{P}[\mathsf{Bac}_{\varepsilon}] \displaystyle\leq\mathbbm{P}[\ell_{1}\cap 5\sqrt{\varepsilon}\mathbbm{D}\neq% \emptyset]+\mathbbm{P}[\ell_{1}\cap 5\sqrt{\varepsilon}\mathbbm{D}=\emptyset,% \mathsf{Bac}_{\varepsilon}]

where the last equality follows from the rotational invariance of B[0,\tau_{\mathbbm{D}}]. The result then follows from combining (4.1), (4.2), (4.3) and (4.4). ∎
