Minuscule Coxeter Dressians

Andreas Gross Institut für Mathematik, Goethe–Universität Frankfurt, 60325 Frankfurt am Main, Germany gross@math.uni-frankfurt.de ,  Kevin Kühn Technische Universität Berlin, 10587 Berlin, Germany kuehn@math.tu-berlin.de  and  Dante Luber Universität Paderborn, 33098 Paderborn, Germany & Queen Mary University of London, E14NS London, United Kingdom dluber@math.uni-paderborn.de
Abstract.

In this extended abstract, we study special tropical prevarieties which we call Coxeter Dressians. They arise from equations capturing a generalization of valuated symmetric basis exchange for Coxeter matroids. In particular, we study subdivisions of the associated Coxeter matroid polytopes. We show that the subdivisions induced by points of the Coxeter Dressian consist of cells which are strong Coxeter matroidal. This generalizes well-known results in type A to other Lie types. Finally, we implement explicit computations of Coxeter Dressians in OSCAR.

1. Introduction

Tropicalization is a process that famously translates algebro-geometric objects into combinatorial ones. Importantly, it is sensitive to chosen coordinates and embeddings of given varieties. Systems of polynomial equations are ubiquitous throughout the disciplines of algebra, geometry, and adjacent fields. In this project, we are interested in tropicalizing so-called strong exchange equations, which appear naturally in representation theory. The spaces they cut out are known as generalized flag varieties.

One such instance is the Grassmann–Plücker equations. It is well-known, that these equations cut out the Grassmannian, which parametrizes subspaces of a given vector space. Its tropical counterpart, the Dressian, parametrizes tropical linear spaces, and is obtained by explicitly tropicalizing the equations. The study of tropical linear spaces, going back to [18], has been proven to be one of the most fruitful endeavors in tropical geometry, with numerous connections to combinatorics, algebra, and biology.

When one studies tropical linear spaces, or valuated matroids, in the background of the theory one always finds the root system A_{n}. An example is the Gel’fand–Serganova theorem, or Speyer’s characterization of valuated matroids [18] of valuations inducing matroidal subdivision of the hypersimplex. The goal of this paper is to go beyond the type A_{n} case, and introduce valuated matroids as well as Dressians for different root systems.

Coxeter matroids are generalizations of matroids which arise as collections of cosets in W/P, where W is a reflection group of any Lie type, and P is a standard parabolic subgroup. We study Coxeter matroids which possess the strong exchange property, which captures the symmetric exchange property of usual matroids. We further restrict to a special subclass, those Coxeter matroids which are said to be minuscule. Minuscule strong exchange Coxeter matroids are the most faithful incarnation of usual matroids outside Lie type A.

For us, the relevant tropical prevarieties are defined by the equations which recently appeared in [6]. We call the prevarieties of interest Coxeter Dressians. Let \mathbb{T}\coloneqq\mathbb{R}\cup\{\infty\} be the semifield of tropical numbers with \min-notation. For minuscule W/P, the Coxeter Dressian is denoted \operatorname{Dr}(W,P)\subseteq\mathbb{T}^{W/P}. For each W/P, these defining equations are obtained by tropicalizing polynomials which generate the ideal of the related generalized flag variety [6]. When feasible, we compute and study concrete examples of these prevarieties. Every Coxeter matroid admits an encoding as a polytope. We study the tropical geometry associated with regular polyhedral subdivisions into cells which are again Coxeter matroidal. Our main theorem is as follows.

Theorem A (Theorem 3.3).

Let (W,P) be minuscule, and \mu\in\operatorname{Dr}(W,P) be a point in the corresponding Coxeter Dressian. Then \mu induces a subdivision of its matroid polytope into strong Coxeter matroid polytopes.

Theorem A holds for all Lie types admitting minuscule matroids. That is, for types A,B,C,D,E_{6},E_{7}. We show that, in previously unknown cases for types D and E_{6}, the converse holds. We give a counter example to the converse for type B. For E_{7}, we show that a proper subset of the strong exchange equations is sufficient to detect strong matroidal subdivisions.

Simplifying systems equations can be crucial for explicit computations. In the A_{n} case, it is well-known that the 3-term Grassmann–Plücker relations imply the other relations [10, 18]. A similar statement is known in the type D_{n} [16]. We prove the type B_{n} analog.

Theorem B (Theorem 4.9).

Let \mu\in\mathbb{T}^{2^{[n]}}, such that \operatorname{supp}(\mu) is a strong Coxeter matroid of type B_{n}. Then \mu belongs to the Coxeter Dressian if and only if \mu satisfies the 4-term strong exchange equations.

As the strong exchange equations reflect the structure of the associated Coxeter matroid polytopes, it is natural to study Coxeter Dressians and valuations via direct computation. Using OSCAR [14], we implement code to compute the polytopes and equations, test if a height function is contained in the Coxeter Dressian, and determine if a subdivision is Coxeter matroidal. We directly compute special cases of Coxeter Dressians in type B_{n} for n\in\{3,4\}, and type D_{n} for n\in\{5,6\}. Furthermore, we compare these prevarieties to the secondary fans of the relevant polytopes, and compute the f-vectors. Finally, we perform an exhaustive computation to verify that strong exchange holds for all E_{7} minuscule Coxeter matroids. Our computations can be found at the following github repository:

https://github.com/danteluber/strong_coxeter_dressians

For this extended abstract, we assume some knowledge of regular subdivisions and secondary fans. The standard reference is [9]. For all aspects of tropical geometry, we point to [12, 13]. Our reference for (Coxeter) matroid theory is [3].

2. Coxeter Matroids and their polytopes

We defer to Chapter 5 of [3] for the formal definitions concerning (classifications of) root systems, reflection groups, and general cryptomorphisms for Coxeter matroids. However, we remind the reader that root system are special collections of vectors corresponding to mirrors over hyperplanes in \mathbb{R}^{n} with deep connections to representation theory. Any root system \Phi gives rise to a Weyl group W, acting on Euclidean space as a finite reflection group. Any choice of simple roots \Phi^{s}=\{s_{1},\dots,s_{n}\} then yields generators of W, i.e., W=\langle s_{1},\dots,s_{n}\rangle. When we refer to a Weyl group, we implicitly assume the datum of its root system is known. Finally, every irreducible root system is uniquely classified by type A_{n},B_{n},C_{n},\dots (etc). So when we say (for example) W=W(B_{n}), we mean that W is a reflection group associated with the type B_{n} root system, and that W is generated by n simple reflections within \Phi^{s}.

Now, let W=\langle s_{1},\dots,s_{n}\mid s_{i}\in\Phi^{s}\rangle, and consider P=\langle s_{i_{1}},\dots,s_{i_{k}}\rangle. Let v\in V\setminus\{0\} be such that \operatorname{stab}_{W}(v)=P. That is, \sigma\cdot v=v if and only if \sigma\in P. Then we identify every element of the orbit W\cdot v with a unique coset in W/P. For any \mathcal{M}\subseteq W/P, we generate the polytope

\Delta(\mathcal{M})=\mathrm{conv}(\{\sigma\cdot v\mid\sigma P\in\mathcal{M}\}).

The vertices of \Delta(W/P) are identified with the cosets, and every edge of \Delta(W/P) is parallel to an element of \Phi. More generally, \mathcal{M}\subseteq W/P is a Coxeter matroid if every edge of \Delta(\mathcal{M}) is parallel to an element of \Phi. Throughout this document, we may interchangeably think of an element A\in\mathcal{M} as a coset or a vertex of the associated polytope. In line with usual matroid theory, when \mathcal{M} is a Coxeter matroid, it’s elements will be referred to as bases.

2.1. Minuscule Coxeter Matroids and the Strong Exchange Property

Let W be a Weyl group associated to the root system \Phi, P\leq W parabolic, and \mathcal{M}\subseteq W/P. Then \mathcal{M} possesses the strong exchange property if for all A,B\in\mathcal{M}, there exists a reflection s\in\Phi such that the mirror defined by s separates A and B, and sA,sB\in\mathcal{M}. Observe that this definition mimics the symmetric exchange property for usual matroids. Any collection of cosets with the strong exchange property is a Coxeter matroid [3, Theorem 6.1.2], but the converse fails [3, 4.2.4]. Finally, the Bruhat order is a partial ordering on W which extends to W/P. When the resulting poset is a lattice, then P, is said to be minuscule [15]. In that case, P is always maximal. That is, P=P_{r}\coloneqq\langle s_{1},\dots\hat{s}_{r},\dots,s_{n}\rangle.

Remark 2.1.

Minuscule parabolic subgroups may also be defined in terms of fundamental weights coming from representation theory. The quotient \mathbb{G}/\mathbb{P} of a reductive group by a parabolic subgroup is naturally a projective variety. For minuscule representations, the ambient projective space of this variety naturally comes with a basis indexed by the cosets of the Weyl group. This allows one to concretely write equations in explicit coordinates that cut out \mathbb{G}/\mathbb{P} [17].

2.2. Set-theoretic Identification of Cosets

In the types A_{n-1},B_{n},C_{n}, and D_{n} with minuscule parabolic P one can identify cosets in W/P with subsets of [n]. We may identify the Weyl group W(A_{n-1}) with the symmetric group S_{n}. The generating set is that of neighbor transpositions s_{i}=(i,i+1), and all P_{r}=\langle s_{1},\dots,\hat{s}_{r},\dots,s_{n}\rangle=\operatorname{stab}_{% S_{n}}([r]) are minuscule. We then identify any coset wP_{r} with w([r])\in 2^{[n]} for w\in S_{n}. Moreover, under this identification, the polytope \Delta(A_{n}/P_{r}) is identified with the (r,n)-hypersimplex \Delta(r,n)=\mathrm{conv}\{e_{A}\mid A\in\binom{[n]}{r}\}.

The Weyl group of B_{n} and C_{n} is the hyperoctahedral group, i.e., the group of permutations of [n]\sqcup-[n] commuting with the involution i\mapsto-i. As generators we have s_{i}=(i,i+1)(-i,-(i+1)) for i=1,\dots,n-1, and s_{n}=(n,-n). The only minuscule parabolic subgroup of B_{n} is P_{n}=\langle s_{1},\dots,s_{n-1}\rangle=\operatorname{stab}_{W(B_{n})}(-[n]). We now identify cosets in W(B_{n})/P with subsets of [n] via wP\mapsto w(-[n])\cap[n]. Hence, the polytope \Delta(B_{n}/P_{n})=\mathrm{conv}\{e_{A}-(-\frac{1}{2},\dots,-\frac{1}{2})\mid A% \in 2^{[n]}\} is exactly the shifted unit n-cube.

2.3. Strong Exchange Equations

Let (W,P) be minuscule, and \mathcal{M}\subseteq W/P. Let \mu_{\mathcal{M}}\in\{0,\infty\}^{W/P} such that \mu_{\mathcal{M}}(A)=0 if A\in\mathcal{M} and \mu_{\mathcal{M}}(A)=\infty otherwise. Recently, in [6] the authors introduced collections of tropical equations \mathcal{F} such that the minimum of f\in\mathcal{F} is attained at least twice when evaluated at \mu_{\mathcal{M}} if and only if \mathcal{M} is a strong Coxeter matroid. These equations are square-free, quadratic, and reflect the polyhedral structure of \Delta(W/P), where the (degree-2)-monomials correspond to pairs of antipodes on a face. These equations are similar in spirit to those studied in [1, 2], however the latter works only cover usual matroids, and consider different hyperfields to detect matroidal properties.

3. The Coxeter Dressian

In this section, we will introduce valuated Coxeter matroids as well as the spaces parameterizing them. Recall that \mathbb{T} denotes the tropical \min-semifield with operations denoted by \oplus and \odot.

Definition 3.1.

Let W be a Weyl group and P a minuscule parabolic subgroup. Let \mathcal{F} be the collection of associated strong exchange equations (see Equations 3, 4, 5, 6, 7). The (strong) Coxeter Dressian of W/P is the tropical prevariety

\text{Dr}(W,P)=\bigcap_{f\in\mathcal{F}}V^{\operatorname{trop}}(f)\subseteq% \mathbb{T}^{W/P}.

We will call points \mu\in\operatorname{Dr}(W,P) valuated Coxeter matroids. Usually, to declutter notation, we will write the Coxeter Dressian, for example, as \operatorname{Dr}(A_{n},P_{r}).

Definition 3.2.

Let E\subset\mathbb{R}^{n} be finite. We say that a set \mathcal{F}\subseteq\mathbb{T}[x_{e}|e\in E] of tropical polynomials with trivial coefficients is affinely invariant if for any function \mu:E\to\mathbb{T} and for for all affine linear functions \varphi:\mathbb{R}^{n}\to\mathbb{R} we have

\mu\in V^{\operatorname{trop}}(\mathcal{F})\text{ if and only if }\mu+\varphi|% _{E}\in V^{\operatorname{trop}}(\mathcal{F})\,.
Theorem 3.3.

Let E\subset\mathbb{R}^{n} be finite, and \mu\colon E\to\mathbb{T}, with induced subdivision S(\mu) of \mathrm{conv}(\operatorname{supp}(\mu)). Let \mathcal{F}\subseteq\mathbb{T}[x_{e}|e\in E] be an affinely invariant set of tropical polynomials with trivial coefficients. If \mu\in V^{\operatorname{trop}}(\mathcal{F})\subseteq\mathbb{T}^{E}, then for any cell C\in S(\mu), the function

\displaystyle\mu_{C}\colon E\to\mathbb{T},\quad e\mapsto\begin{cases}% \hidden@noalign{}\textstyle 0\ \ \ \text{ if }e\in C\\ \hidden@noalign{}\textstyle\infty\,\text{ else}\end{cases}

satisfies all tropical equations in \mathcal{F}. In other words, \mu_{C}\in V^{\operatorname{trop}}(\mathcal{F)}.

  • Proof.

    Consider a cell C of S(\mu). Let \tilde{\mu}=\mu+\varphi for some affine linear function \varphi such that \tilde{\mu}|_{C}=0 and \tilde{\mu}|_{S\setminus C}>0. Since \mathcal{F} is affinely invariant, \tilde{\mu}\in V^{\operatorname{trop}}(\mathcal{F}). Since \mu,\tilde{\mu} only differ by an affine linear function, their induced subdivisions agree. Let f\in\mathcal{F}, then f=\bigoplus_{\alpha}x^{\odot\alpha} for finitely many \alpha\in\mathbb{N}^{E}. Since this minimum is attained at least twice, when plugging in \tilde{\mu}, there are two cases:

    • –

      If the evaluation f(\tilde{\mu})=0, then there are at least two monomials of f supported on C. In that case, surely f(\mu_{C}) = 0, and the minimum is attained at these monomials. Thus, \mu_{C}\in V^{\operatorname{trop}}(f).

    • –

      If f(\tilde{\mu})>0, then all monomials of f contain a variable in E\setminus C. It follows, that f(\mu_{C})=\infty, and hence trivially \mu_{C} tropically satisfies f.

    ∎

Theorem 3.4.

For all minuscule types A_{n},B_{n},C_{n},D_{n},E_{6},E_{7}, the set of strong exchange equations are affinely invariant. Thus, by Theorem 3.3, all height functions V(\Delta(W/P))\to\mathbb{T} satisfying the respective equations induce strong matroidal subdivisions.

  • Proof.

    We prove affine invariance case by case in Section 4. More specifically, we deal with cases where valuations are not already well studied. We do so via Propositions 4.1 (type (B_{n},P_{n})), 4.4 (type (D_{n},P_{1})), 4.12 (type (E_{7},P_{1})). Proofs for the other cases can be done in a similar fashion, but are omitted here. From affine invariance, the indicator vectors of the cells satisfy respective strong exchange equations, hence by [6, Theorem 1.1], the cells are strong matroid polytopes. ∎

4. The Minuscule Types

In this section we will explicitly look at tropical strong exchange equations individually for each minuscule Lie type. These equations were systematically analyzed in [6] over the Boolean semifield.

4.1. Type A_{n}

As mentioned in the introduction, valuations of type A Coxeter matroids have been extensively studied even outside the minuscule case [18, 5, 11]. For the equations corresponding to usual matroids, we defer to [13, Chapter 4]. The type A strong exchange equations are exactly the Grassmann–Plücker equations. They are affinely invariant, but we will omit a proof in this abstract.

4.2. Type B_{n}

Recall that in type B_{n}, the Weyl group is the hyperoctahedral group, and the only minuscule parabolic subgroup is P_{n}=\langle s_{1},\dots,s_{n-1}\rangle=\operatorname{stab}_{B_{n}}(-[n]). We identify cosets wP_{n}\in B_{n}/P_{n} with subsets of {[n]} via wP_{n}\mapsto w(-[n])\cap[n]. Fixing (B_{n},P_{n}), the strong exchange equations are

(3) \displaystyle f_{I,J}^{B}\coloneqq\begin{cases}\hidden@noalign{}\textstyle% \bigoplus_{i\in I\triangle J}x_{I\triangle i}\odot x_{J\triangle i}\hskip 56.0% 51929pt\text{ if }|I\triangle J|\equiv 0\mod 2\\ \hidden@noalign{}\textstyle x_{I}\odot x_{J}\oplus\bigoplus_{i\in I\triangle J% }x_{I\triangle i}\odot x_{J\triangle i}\quad\text{ if }|I\triangle J|\equiv 1% \mod 2,\end{cases}

for all I,J\subseteq[n] with |I\triangle J|\geq 3. They are tropicalizations of quadratic algebraic equations cutting out the type B_{n} minuscule flag variety.

Proposition 4.1.

The type B_{n} strong exchange equations 3 are affinely invariant.

  • Proof.

    Let \mu:2^{[n]}\to\mathbb{T} be arbitrary, and \varphi:\mathbb{R}^{n}\to\mathbb{R} be affine linear. We consider the variables as vertices of the n-cube Q_{n}=[-\frac{1}{2},\frac{1}{2}]^{n}. We fix I,J\subseteq[n] with |I\triangle J|\geq 3. For each monomial x_{I\triangle i}\odot x_{J\triangle i} in f^{B}_{I,J}, evaluating at \mu+\varphi|_{V(Q_{n})} yields \mu_{I\setminus i}+\mu_{J\cup i}+(\varphi(e_{I})+\varphi(e_{J})-\varphi(0)), the last term being independent of i. The same holds for x_{I}\odot x_{J}, in the case |I\triangle J|\equiv 1\mod 2. Thus \mu attains the minimum at least twice if and only if \mu+\varphi|_{V(\Delta(r,n))} does. ∎

It is now natural to ask, whether type B_{n} behaves as nicely as type A_{n}, and the converse of Theorem 3.4 also holds. This turns out to be wrong already for n=3, as the following example shows.

Example 4.2.

We get a single strong exchange equation in type B_{3}, which is supported on the entire 3-cube and given by

f^{B}_{\emptyset,[3]}=x_{\emptyset}\odot x_{123}\oplus x_{1}\odot x_{23}\oplus x% _{2}\odot x_{13}\oplus x_{3}\odot x_{12}.

Some functions on the 3-cube induce strong matroidal subdivisions, but are not in the Coxeter Dressian. One such example is:

(\mu_{\emptyset},\mu_{1},\mu_{2},\mu_{3},\mu_{12},\mu_{13},\mu_{23},\mu_{123})% =(0,0,0,-1,2,1,1,0)\,.

There are 5 maximal cells in the induced subdivision, each of which are tetrahedra. Notice that none of these cells contain any antipodes. We visualize the subdivision in Figure 1. However, by direct computation, we observe that there is an element of \operatorname{Dr}(B_{3},P_{3}) that induces this subdivision.

Lemma 4.3.

Let \mu\in\mathbb{R}^{2^{[n]}} satisfy the all strong exchange equations supported on the 3-dimensional faces of the 4-cube. Then \mu is already a strong valuated Coxeter matroid.

4.3. Type C_{n}

The only minuscule parabolic of type C is P_{1}. Cosets wP_{1}\in W(C_{n})/P_{1} are identified with [n]\sqcup-[n] via wP_{1}\mapsto w(1). Hence, the polytope associated to (C_{n},P_{1}) is given by the cross polytope

\Diamond_{n}=\mathrm{conv}(\pm e_{i}:i\in[n]).

All pairs (\pm e_{i},\pm e_{j}) for i\neq j form edges on \Diamond_{n}. On the other hand, 2e_{i} is an element of the type C_{n} root system. In other words, all possible subpolytopes of \Diamond_{n} are strong matroids, and all subdivisions of \Diamond_{n} are strong matroidal. In particular, there are no strong exchange type C_{n} equations, and the Coxeter Dressian \operatorname{Dr}(C_{n},P_{1}) is exactly the secondary fan of \Diamond_{n}.

4.4. Type D_{n}

Recall that in type D_{n}, the Weyl group is the intersection of the alternating group with the hyperoctahedral group. There are three minuscule parabolic subgroups P_{1},P_{n-1}, and P_{n}. Note that the last two are isomorphic as subgroups of D_{n}, and they were studied in [16].

4.4.1. Minuscule Parabolic P_{1} in type D

Note that W(D_{n})/P_{1}\cong W(C_{n})/P_{1}, hence the polytope that arises in this situation is again the cross-polytope \Diamond_{n} of Section 4.3. However, 2e_{i} is not an element of the D_{n} root system, so the segment connecting e_{i},-e_{i} is not an admissible edge of a type D_{n} matroidal subdivision of \Diamond_{n}. It follows in a straightforward way that any subdivision of \Diamond_{n} that is not a triangulation is strong matroidal. The singular strong exchange equation defining \operatorname{Dr}(D_{n},P_{1}) is of the form:

(4) \displaystyle f^{D}_{\Diamond_{n}}=\bigoplus^{n}_{i=1}x_{i}\odot x_{-i}\,.
Proposition 4.4.

The type D_{n} strong exchange equation 4 is affinely invariant.

  • Proof.

    Consider f^{E_{7}}, and \mu\in\mathbb{T}^{V(\Diamond_{n})}. For \varphi:\mathbb{R}^{n}\to\mathbb{R} affine linear, we have that x_{i}\odot x_{-i} evaluated at \mu+\varphi|_{V(\Diamond_{n})} yields \mu_{i}+\mu_{-i}+2\varphi(0). Therefore, \mu attains the minimum at least twice if and only if \mu+\varphi does. ∎

Proposition 4.5.

A height function \mu induces a type D strong matroidal subdivision of \Diamond_{n} if and only if \mu\in\operatorname{Dr}(D_{n},P_{1}).

  • Proof.

    One direction follows immediately from Proposition 4.4. For the other, suppose the \mu induced subdivision is strong matroidal, and let \Delta(\mathcal{M}) be a maximal cell. Then \Delta(\mathcal{M})=C_{v},

    C_{v}=\mathrm{conv}(e_{i}:\langle v,e_{i}\rangle)+\mu_{i}\leq\langle v,e_{k}% \rangle+\mu_{k}\text{ where }i,k\in[n]\cup[-n]).

    Now, observe that for C_{v} to be full dimensional, C_{v} must use at least n+1 vertices of \Diamond_{n}. As a consequence, we must have some some i\in[n] where e_{i},e_{-i}\in C_{v}. Furthermore, since \Delta(\mathcal{M})=C_{v} is a strong matroid, we have at least one j such that e_{j},e_{-j}\in C_{v}. This leads to the following collection of relations:

    \displaystyle\langle v,e_{\pm i}\rangle+\mu_{\pm i}=\langle v,e_{\pm j}\rangle% +\mu_{\pm j}\leq\langle v,e_{\pm k}\rangle+\mu_{\pm k}\text{ for any }k\in[n].

    From these, one can deduce \mu_{i}+\mu_{-i}=\mu_{j}+\mu_{-j}\leq\mu_{k}+\mu_{-k}\text{ for any $k$}. ∎

Proposition 4.6.

For n\in\{5,6\}, the Coxeter Dressian \operatorname{Dr}(D_{n},P_{1}) is isomorphic to a proper subfan of the secondary fan of \Diamond_{n}. That is, the coarsest polyhedral structure of the former agrees with the fan structure of the latter, the Coxeter Dressian.

  • Proof.

    We verify this computationally. See the related notebook on the github repository. ∎

It follows from Proposition 4.6, and the fact that any subdivision of \Diamond_{n} which isn’t a triangulation is strong matroidal, that \operatorname{Dr}(D_{n},P_{1}) is isomorphic to the subfan of the secondary fan consisting of codimension-1 cones.

4.4.2. Minuscule Parabolic P_{n}

We identify cosets A\in W(D_{n})/P_{n} with even subsets of {[n]} via A\mapsto A(-[n])\cap[n]. In analogy to the B_{n} case, the corresponding polytope is the demicube, the subpolytope of the cube with even vertices. We note that the parabolics P_{n-1} and P_{n} are isomorphic as subgroups of W(D_{n}), hence we only consider the latter case. The strong exchange equations are given by

(5) \displaystyle f_{I,J}^{D}\coloneqq\bigoplus_{i\in I\triangle J}x_{I\triangle i% }\odot x_{J\triangle i}

for I,J\subseteq[n] with |I\triangle J|\geq 4 and I,J of odd cardinality. Affine invariance follows exactly like in 4.1. There is a simple construction relating valuated strong Coxeter matroids of type B_{n} to those of type D_{n+1}, with minuscule parabolic P_{n+1}.

Proposition 4.7.

The embedding \mathbb{T}^{2^{[n]}}\hookrightarrow\mathbb{T}^{2^{[n+1]}} given by

2^{[n]}\longrightarrow 2^{[n+1]}\quad A\mapsto\overline{A}=\begin{cases}A&% \text{ if }|A|\text{ even}\\ A\cup\{n+1\}&\text{ if }|A|\text{ odd.}\\ \end{cases}

induces a linear isomorphism \operatorname{Dr}(B_{n},P_{n})\cong\operatorname{Dr}(D_{n+1},P_{n+1}).

  • Proof.

    We first show that type B_{n} strong exchange equations are in bijection to type D_{n+1} strong exchange equations via f_{I,J}^{B}\mapsto f_{I^{\prime},J^{\prime}}^{D}. Here I^{\prime}=I\cup\{n+1\} if |I| even, and I^{\prime}=I if |I| odd, similarly, J^{\prime}. Bijectivity follows from the fact that I^{\prime}, J^{\prime} are always of odd cardinality, and |I\triangle J|\geq 3 if and only if |I^{\prime}\triangle J^{\prime}|\geq 4.

    Let \mu\in\operatorname{Dr}(B_{n},P_{n}), and \overline{\mu}\in\mathbb{T}^{2^{[n+1]}} its corresponding image. We now show that \mu satisfies f_{I,J}^{B} if and only if \overline{\mu} satisfies f_{I^{\prime},J^{\prime}}^{D}. If both I,J are of odd cardinality, then the respective equations are the same, and there is nothing to show. If I is odd, and J is even, then I^{\prime}=I, and J^{\prime}=J\cup\{n+1\}. In that case, I^{\prime}\triangle J^{\prime}=I\triangle J\cup\{n+1\} is of odd cardinality. Hence, for all i\in I\triangle J, we have I\triangle\{i\} is even, and \overline{J\triangle i}=J^{\prime}\triangle i. Finally, for i=\{n+1\}, we have I\triangle i=I, and J=J^{\prime}\triangle i, finishing the proof for the case |I| even, |J| odd. Lastly, let I,J both be even. Then I\triangle J=I^{\prime}\triangle J^{\prime}, and for all i\in I\triangle J we have \overline{I\triangle i}=I^{\prime}\triangle i, finishing the proof. ∎

We note that the above is already true algebraically, as the same morphism over any field maps the minuscule type B_{n} flag variety isomorphically onto the even component of the spinor variety. There is also an equivalent map \operatorname{Dr}(B_{n},P_{n})\cong\operatorname{Dr}(D_{n+1},P_{n}), mapping subsets of [n] to odd subsets of [n+1]. As the even and odd components of the spinor variety are isomorphic, this is not surprising. The following can be deduced immediately from Proposition 4.7 and [16, Theorem 4.6].

Corollary 4.8.

For n\leq 4, the Coxeter Dressian \operatorname{Dr}(B_{n},P_{n}) is the tropicalization of the minuscule type B_{n} flag variety. For n\geq 6, it is strictly larger than the tropicalization. In particular, not every valuated Coxeter matroid of type B_{n} is realizable for n\geq 6.

Theorem 4.9.

Let \mu\in\mathbb{T}^{2^{[n]}}, such that \operatorname{supp}(\mu) is a strong Coxeter matroid of type B_{n} . Then the following statements are equivalent:

  1. (a)

    \mu is in the Coxeter Dressian,

  2. (b)

    \mu satisfies the four term strong exchange equations f_{I,J}^{B} for |I\triangle J|\leq 4.

Moreover, if all entries of \mu are finite, in (b) it suffices to only consider the case |I\triangle J|\leq 3.

  • Proof.

    The statement (a) \Rightarrow (b) holds by definition. For the other direction, we need to show that \mu satisfies all strong exchange equations f^{B}_{I,J}. Consider the image \overline{\mu} from Proposition 4.7. By [16, Theorem 5.1], \overline{\mu} satisfies the type D_{n+1} strong exchange equations if and only if it satisfies the respective four-term strong exchange equations. Hence \overline{\mu}\in\operatorname{Dr}(D_{n+1},P_{n+1}), and we conclude \mu\in\operatorname{Dr}(B_{n},P_{n}).

    Let additionally all entries of \mu be finite. Restricting \mu to the various 4-cubes and applying Lemma 4.3 we obtain that \mu satisfies all equations f_{I,J}^{B} with |I\triangle J|=4 if it does so for all |I\triangle J|=3. ∎

Example 4.10.

The point \mu\in\mathbb{T}^{2^{[4]}} given by \mu_{\emptyset}=\mu_{1234}=\mu_{12}=0, \mu_{34}=1, and \mu_{A}=\infty for all other A\in 2^{[n]} does not satisfy f^{B}_{1,234}. Hence, it is not in the Coxeter Dressian However, it satisfies all equations supported on proper 3-faces of the 4-cube.

4.5. Type E_{6}

In type E_{6}, the two minuscule parabolic subgroups are P_{1} and P_{6}. Since P_{1}\cong P_{6}\cong W(D_{5}), we have W(E_{6})/P_{1}\cong W(E_{6})/P_{6} as sets with W-action. We thus consider both cases simultaneously. The polytope is commonly referred to as 2_{21}=\Delta(E_{6}/P_{1}) [7]. The face structure of 2_{21} is studied in [8]. We recall the relevant facts. The polytope 2_{21} is 6 dimensional and has 27 vertices, each at most edge-distance 2 from each other. Each distance 2 pair of vertices defines a \Diamond_{5} cross polytope in the 5-skeleton of 2_{21}, of which there are 27. All other facets are simplices. For each distance-2 pair (A,B), we get a strong exchange equation

(6) \displaystyle f^{E_{6}}_{A,B}=x_{A}\odot x_{B}\oplus\bigoplus^{4}_{i=1}x_{C_{i% }}\odot x_{C^{\prime}_{i}},

where the pairs (A,B) and (C_{i},C^{\prime}_{i}) for i\in[4] are antipodes of the corresponding cross polytope. All strong exchange equations for minuscule E_{6} arise in this way. By Proposition 4.4, the respective equations are affinely invariant.

Proposition 4.11.

A height function \mu induces a strong matroidal subdivision of 2_{21} if and only if \mu\in\operatorname{Dr}(E_{6},P_{1}).

  • Proof.

    One direction follows from affine invariance. For the other, let f_{\Diamond} correspond to a D_{5} cross polytope \Diamond in the 5-skeleton of 2_{21}, and consider the restriction of the subdivision induced by \mu to \Diamond. If the minimum on f_{\Diamond} were unique, Proposition 4.6 tells us that there is a maximal cell of the restriction with peerless antipodes (A,B). Since all possible peers for (A,B) in 2_{21} correspond to entries of f_{\Diamond}, (A,B) form peerless antipodes of some maximal cell in the global subdivision. The result follows from the contrapositive. ∎

4.6. Type E_{7}

In type E_{7} we only have a single minuscule parabolic P_{1}, and the polytope is commonly known as 3_{21}=\Delta(E_{7}/P_{1}) [8]. The polytope is 7 dimensional, with 56 vertices with maximum edge distance 3 between pairs. Each vertex has 27 neighbors. Each distance 2 pair induces a \Diamond_{6} facet, and distance 3 pairs are of the form (v,-v). Since each element A has a unique distance 3 counterpart B, we just write B=-A. The strong exchange equations come in two forms. we have an equation for each \Diamond_{6} facet, one supported on all pairs of antipodes of 3_{21}

(7) \displaystyle f^{E_{7}}_{A,B} \displaystyle=x_{A}\odot x_{B}\;\bigoplus_{i=1}^{5}x_{C_{i}}\odot x_{C^{\prime% }_{i}} \displaystyle\text{when }d(A,B)=2,
\displaystyle f^{E_{7}} \displaystyle=\bigoplus_{A\in W/P}x_{A}\odot x_{-A}.
Proposition 4.12.

The type E_{7} strong exchange equations 7 are affinely invariant.

  • Proof.

    Since the equations f^{E_{7}}_{A,B} are the exact equations 4 on cross polytopes, the corresponding statement follows from Proposition 4.4. Consider f^{E_{7}}, and \mu\in\mathbb{T}^{V(3_{21})}. For \varphi:\mathbb{R}^{8}\to\mathbb{R} affine linear, we have that x_{A}\odot x_{-A} evaluated at \mu+\varphi|_{V(3_{21})} yields \mu_{A}+\mu_{-A}+2\varphi(0). Therefore, \mu attains the minimum at least twice if and only if \mu+\varphi does. ∎

Over the Krasner hyperfield, the strong exchange equations (7) are equivalent to the symmetric exchange axiom of Borovik, Gelfand, and White [4], which they conjectured to be equivalent to the Coxeter matroid axioms (e.g. the maximality property) in all minuscule types [3, Section 6.16]. By means of an exhaustive search, we prove that this conjecture holds in E_{7}.

Theorem 4.13.

All Coxeter matroids of type (E_{7},P_{7}) satisfy the strong exchange condition.

In combination with known results in the other types we conclude that in all types except B_{n}, all minuscule Coxeter matroids satisfy the strong exchange axiom. Moreover, Theorem 4.13 implies that the relations on the proper faces of 3_{21} are already sufficient to guarantee a subdivision consists of strong Coxeter matroid polytopes.

Theorem 4.14.

A height function \mu induces a strong matroidal subdivision of 3_{21} if and only if it is contained in \bigcap_{(A,B)}V^{\text{Trop}}(f^{E_{7}}_{A,B}), where (A,B) runs over all pairs of elements of W(E_{7})/P_{7} with d(A,B)=2.

However, it is still open whether all \mu satisfying the equivalent conditions of Theorem 4.14 are in the Dressian.

Conjecture 1.

The support of \operatorname{Dr}(E_{7},P_{7}) is equal to the support of \bigcap V^{\text{Trop}}(f^{E_{7}}_{A,B}).
