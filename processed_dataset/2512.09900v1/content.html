Random walks on cocompact Fuchsian and Kleinian groups

Nikolay Bogachev Department of Computer and Mathematical Sciences, University of Toronto Scarborough, 1095 Military Trail, Toronto, ON M1C 1A3, Canada n.bogachev@utoronto.ca ,  Petr Kosenko Department of Mathematics, University of British Columbia, Canada pkosenko@math.ubc.ca  and  Giulio Tiozzo Department of Mathematics, University of Toronto, 40 St George Street, Toronto ON, M5S 2E4, Canada tiozzo@math.utoronto.ca
Abstract.

The question of the singularity at infinity of the hitting measure of random walks has a long history, originating from the work of Furstenberg in the 1960s. In 2011, Kaimanovich and Le Prince conjectured that the hitting measure of any finitely supported random walk on a discrete subgroup \Gamma of \mathrm{SL}_{N}(\mathbb{R}) is singular at infinity with respect to the Lebesgue measure. Using algebraic and geometric convergence and hyperbolic Dehn filling, we prove the singularity conjecture for certain measures on “most” cocompact Fuchsian and Kleinian groups.

1. Introduction

This paper concerns hitting measures of finitely supported random walks on finitely generated discrete subgroups of isometries of hyperbolic spaces \mathbb{H}^{d}. This subject is currently of growing interest due to the rich interplay between geometric group theory, geometric topology, probability and dynamics. One of the central questions in this field is the singularity conjecture of Kaimanovich and Le Prince [19], which can be broadly formulated for discrete subgroups \Gamma<\mathrm{SL}_{N}(\mathbb{R}) as follows.

Conjecture 1.1 (Kaimanovich and Le Prince [19]).

The hitting measure of any random walk finitely supported on a discrete subgroup \Gamma<\mathrm{SL}_{N}(\mathbb{R}) is singular at infinity with respect to the Lebesgue measure.

This conjecture has, in fact, a very long history originating from the work of Furstenberg [11, 12] who proved that for any lattice \Gamma in a semisimple Lie group, there exists an infinitely-supported random walk on \Gamma with finite first moment in the hyperbolic metric such that the hitting measure is absolutely continuous on the boundary.

The singularity conjecture remains widely open even for lattices in Lie groups, including \mathrm{PSL}_{2}(\mathbb{R}), \mathrm{PSL}_{2}(\mathbb{C}), and \mathrm{PO}_{d,1}(\mathbb{R}). We are primarily interested in hyperbolic lattices \Gamma<\mathrm{Isom}(\mathbb{H}^{d})\cong\mathrm{PO}_{d,1}(\mathbb{R}). The orientation-preserving isometry group \mathrm{Isom}^{+}(\mathbb{H}^{d}) of \mathbb{H}^{d} can be identified with \mathrm{PO}^{\circ}_{d,1}(\mathbb{R}), and in low dimensions we have \mathrm{Isom}^{+}(\mathbb{H}^{2})\cong\mathrm{PSL}_{2}(\mathbb{R}) and \mathrm{Isom}^{+}(\mathbb{H}^{3})\cong\mathrm{PSL}_{2}(\mathbb{C}). Discrete subgroups of the latter two Lie groups are called Fuchsian and Kleinian, respectively.

As mentioned above, even in the special case of hyperbolic lattices, Conjecture 1.1 is still open; however, some progress was obtained. The singularity conjecture is proven for non-uniform lattices in \mathrm{PSL}_{2}(\mathbb{R}), see Guivarc’h–Le Jan [16], Deroin–Kleptsyn–Navas [9], and Blachère–Haïssinsky–Mathieu [5]. Recently, Randecker–Tiozzo [29] proved this conjecture for random walks on all non-uniform hyperbolic lattices \Gamma<\mathrm{PO}_{d,1}(\mathbb{R}). A similar result for cocompact Fuchsian groups with centrally symmetric fundamental domains was obtained by Kosenko–Tiozzo [24]. Not that much is known in higher rank either. For example, if G is a semisimple Lie group with Kazhdan’s property (T), \Gamma<G is an infinite-covolume discrete Zariski-dense subgroup, and \mu is a symmetric probability measure on \Gamma such that \langle\mathrm{supp}\,\mu\rangle=\Gamma, then the Furstenberg measure \nu on G/P is singular with respect to the Lebesgue measure; see recent preprints Lee–Tiozzo–Van Limbeek [25] and Kim–Zimmer [22].

We prove the singularity conjecture for large families of cocompact Fuchsian and Kleinian groups. To formulate the main result, we need to introduce some preliminary information.

The key novel idea of this paper is to geometrically approximate groups for which the singularity conjecture is proven by a sequence of other groups. Our results and methods rely on the theory of small deformations and geometric convergence of finite-volume hyperbolic 3-manifolds. Geometric convergence is equivalent to the convergence in the Chabauty topology of subgroups of \mathrm{PSL}_{2}(\mathbb{C}). On the other hand, small deformations mean the deformations of a certain representation and give rise to the algebraic convergence of representations of a given group. It is well known that among lattices in \mathrm{Isom}(\mathbb{H}^{d}), d\geq 3, only nonuniform lattices in \mathrm{Isom}(\mathbb{H}^{3}) admit small deformations (for uniform lattices it was proved by Weil [33], while for nonuniform lattices it is a result of Garland–Raghunathan [13]).

Nonuniform lattices in \mathrm{Isom}(\mathbb{H}^{3}) are usually not locally rigid, meaning that their small deformations are not conjugate to the initial representation, as was discovered by Thurston. The small deformations we discuss here do not lead, in general, to lattices or even discrete subgroups of \mathrm{Isom}(\mathbb{H}^{3}). However, Thurston’s hyperbolic Dehn filling theorem allows one to obtain lattices under certain parameters of such deformations. We discuss the relation between small deformations, geometric convergence, and hyperbolic Dehn filling in Section 3.2. Moreover, we note that for any geometrically convergent sequence of lattices \Gamma_{n}\to\Gamma one can always choose measures \mu_{n} on \Gamma_{n} and \mu on \Gamma which “are compatible with each other”. This compatibility comes from the hyperbolic Dehn filling on the limit lattice \Gamma; for a precise definition, see Section 3.3. Here, we just highlight the fact that picking a single measure \mu on \Gamma is enough to “translate” it to measures \mu_{n} on all but finitely many sequence elements \Gamma_{n} such that all \mu_{n} and \mu are pairwise compatible. Finally, a measure \mu on a lattice \Gamma is non-degenerate if \Gamma is the group generated by the support of \mu.

Theorem 1.2.

For any geometrically convergent sequence \Gamma_{n}\to\Gamma of torsion-free lattices \Gamma_{n},\Gamma<\mathrm{PSL}_{2}(\mathbb{C}), where \Gamma_{n} are not pairwise conjugate, equipped with any sequence of compatible, non-degenerate, finitely-supported measures \mu_{n} and \mu, respectively, there exists a number N=N(\mu)>0 such that for all n>N the hitting measure \nu_{\mu_{n}} of the random walk on \Gamma_{n} driven by \mu_{n} is singular with respect to the Lebesgue measure on the ideal boundary \partial_{\infty}\mathbb{H}^{3}\cong\mathbb{S}^{2}.

In Section 2.4, we explain how the tools of geometric convergence and hyperbolic Dehn filling work for compact hyperbolic 3-orbifolds, i.e. for cocompact Kleinian groups not necessarily torsion-free. The orbifold version of Theorem 1.2 is the following.

Theorem 1.3.

Let \Gamma<\mathrm{PSL}_{2}(\mathbb{C}) be a non-uniform lattice such that each cusp of the (oriented) orbifold \mathcal{O}=\mathbb{H}^{3}/\Gamma is either a torus \mathbb{T}^{2} or a pillowcase S^{2}(2,2,2,2). Consider a sequence of lattices \Gamma_{n} that geometrically converge to \Gamma. Then for any non-degenerate finitely supported measure \mu on \Gamma there is N>0 such that for all n>N the following conditions hold:

  1. (1)

    The orbifolds \mathcal{O}_{n}=\mathbb{H}^{3}/\Gamma_{n} are obtained by hyperbolic Dehn filling of one of the cusps of \mathcal{O}.

  2. (2)

    There exist finitely supported measures \mu_{n} on \Gamma_{n} compatible with the measure \mu on \Gamma.

  3. (3)

    The hitting measure \nu_{\mu_{n}} of the random walk driven by \mu_{n} is singular with respect to the Lebesgue measure on the ideal boundary \partial_{\infty}\mathbb{H}^{3}\cong\mathbb{S}^{2}.

Proof.

Follows from Section 2.4, including the hyperbolic Dehn filling theorem for orbifolds by Dunbar–Meyerhoff [10], Proposition 3.1, and from the last part of the proof of Theorem 1.2. \scriptstyle\blacksquare

Here we provide a special but very natural version of our main theorem in the setting of hyperbolic reflection groups.

Theorem 1.4.

Let \Sigma be the Coxeter–Vinberg diagram on m vertices of a compact Coxeter polyhedron P\subset\mathbb{H}^{3} with a dihedral angle \pi/n, n\geq 6, at one of its edges e. By varying this parameter n with other dihedral angles remaining fixed, we obtain a sequence of compact Coxeter polyhedra P_{n}\subset\mathbb{H}^{3} whose corresponding cocompact reflection groups \Gamma_{n} (generated by reflections r^{(n)}_{1},\ldots,r^{(n)}_{m} in the walls of P_{n}) satisfy the following conditions.

  1. (1)

    \Gamma_{n}\to\Gamma geometrically, where \Gamma is a cofinite group generated by reflections r_{1},\ldots,r_{m} in the walls of a 1-cusped polyhedron P_{\infty} obtained from P_{n} by contracting the edge e to a point v_{\infty} on the ideal boundary \partial\mathbb{H}^{3}.

  2. (2)

    Assume that the measures \mu_{n} are defined by assigning probability p_{i}>0 to the generating reflection r^{(n)}_{i}, where 1\leq i\leq m and the p_{i} do not depend on n. Then there exists a number N>0 such that for all n>N the hitting measure \nu_{\mu_{n}} of the finitely supported random walk on \Gamma_{n} with distribution \mu_{n} is singular with respect to the Lebesgue measure on the ideal boundary \partial_{\infty}\mathbb{H}^{3}\cong\mathbb{S}^{2}.

In the case of Fuchsian groups, the situation is different. In contrast to the Kleinian groups case, there are, in general, uncountably many deformations of lattices in \mathrm{Isom}(\mathbb{H}^{2}). Moreover, the algebraic and geometric convergence do not coincide even in the case of lattices in \mathrm{Isom}(\mathbb{H}^{2}). See Section 5 for examples.

Theorem 1.5.

Let \Gamma be a non-uniform lattice in \mathrm{PSL}_{2}(\mathbb{R})=\mathrm{Isom}^{+}(\mathbb{H}^{2}), i.e. a cofinite Fuchsian group, and \mu a non-degenerate finitely supported measure on \Gamma. Then for every sequence of cocompact Fuchsian groups \Gamma_{n}=\rho_{n}(\Gamma) algebraically converging to \Gamma=\rho(\Gamma), there exists N\in\mathbb{N} such that for every n>N, the hitting measure \nu_{n}=\nu_{\mu_{n}} of \mu_{n}:=(\rho_{n})_{*}\mu is singular with respect to the Lebesgue measure on \partial_{\infty}\mathbb{H}^{2}\cong\mathbb{S}^{1}.

We also note that there exist many small deformations of cocompact Fuchsian groups that are also cocompact Fuchsian. This allows one to extend Theorem 1.5 to uniform lattices in \mathrm{PSL}_{2}(\mathbb{R}) that algebraically approximate those for which this theorem is proven, and one can thus continue adding more and more groups that satisfy the singularity conjecture.

2. Preliminaries

2.1. Hyperbolic manifolds and orbifolds

The d-dimensional hyperbolic space \mathbb{H}^{d} is the unique connected, simply connected Riemannian manifold of constant negative curvature. It has several useful models, among which we are mostly interested in the hyperboloid model \mathcal{H}^{d} and the upper half-space model \mathcal{U}^{d}.

To describe the hyperboloid model, we consider the Minkowski space \mathbb{R}^{d,1}, i.e. \mathbb{R}^{d+1} equipped with a Lorentzian scalar product (\cdot,\cdot) of signature (d,1). Passing to the standard basis of \mathbb{R}^{d,1}, we can write (x,y)=-x_{0}y_{0}+x_{1}y_{1}+\ldots+x_{d}y_{d}. Then we set

\mathcal{H}^{d}:=\{x\in\mathbb{R}^{d,1}\mid(x,x)=-1,x_{0}>0\},

which is a connected component of the hyperboloid \{x\in\mathbb{R}^{d,1}\mid(x,x)=-1\}. The Riemannian metric tensor g_{\mathcal{H}} is defined as the restriction of the Lorentzian scalar product (\cdot,\cdot) on the tangent bundle T\mathcal{H}^{d}. The hyperbolic distance function \rho_{\mathcal{H}} then satisfies \cosh\rho_{\mathcal{H}}(x,y)=-(x,y). The full isometry group \mathrm{Isom}(\mathbb{H}^{d}) is isomorphic to the index-2 subgroup \mathrm{O}^{\prime}_{d,1}(\mathbb{R}) of \mathrm{O}_{d,1}(\mathbb{R}) defined by the condition that every matrix A\in\mathrm{O}^{\prime}_{d,1}(\mathbb{R}) leaves \mathcal{H}^{d} invariant. This group can also be identified with the projective orthogonal group \mathrm{PO}_{d,1}(\mathbb{R})=\mathrm{O}_{d,1}(\mathbb{R})/\{\pm\mathtt{id}\}. Totally geodesic lines (geodesics) and planes are the intersections of \mathcal{H}^{d} with vector subspaces of \mathbb{R}^{d,1}. Hyperbolic reflections in hyperplanes are linear reflections with respect to hyperplanes of \mathbb{R}^{d,1}.

The upper half-space model \mathcal{U}^{d} is defined as follows:

\mathcal{U}^{d}=\{x=(x_{1},\ldots,x_{d})\in\mathbb{R}^{d}\mid x_{d}>0\}\text{ % with a metric tensor }g_{\mathcal{U}}=\frac{g_{\mathcal{E}}}{x_{d}^{2}},

where g_{\mathcal{E}} is the standard Euclidean metric on \mathbb{R}^{d}. Totally geodesic planes (including one-dimensional lines) are, in this model, either vertical Euclidean half-planes or hemispheres orthogonal to the boundary hyperplane \{x\in\mathbb{R}^{d}\mid x_{d}=0\}.

In dimensions 2 and 3, it is useful to involve the complex plane in this model:

\mathcal{U}^{2}=\{z\in\mathbb{C}\mid\mathtt{Im}(z)>0\}\text{ and }\mathcal{U}^% {3}=\{(z,t)\in\mathbb{C}\times\mathbb{R}_{>0}\}.

The orientation-preserving isometry groups of \mathbb{H}^{2} and \mathbb{H}^{3} are then identified with the groups of fractional linear (Möbius) automorphisms of \mathcal{U}^{2} and \mathbb{C}, respectively: \mathrm{Isom}^{+}(\mathbb{H}^{2})\cong\mathrm{PSL}_{2}(\mathbb{R}) and \mathrm{Isom}^{+}(\mathbb{H}^{3})\cong\mathrm{PSL}_{2}(\mathbb{C}).

Any complete connected hyperbolic d-manifold is a quotient M=\mathbb{H}^{d}/\Gamma, where \Gamma<\mathrm{Isom}(\mathbb{H}^{d}) is a torsion-free discrete subgroup. If \Gamma has finite-order elements, then M is called a hyperbolic orbifold and \Gamma its orbifold fundamental group. The manifold (or orbifold) M has finite volume if and only if \Gamma is a lattice in the real Lie group \mathrm{Isom}(\mathbb{H}^{d}), i.e. \Gamma has finite-volume fundamental domain in \mathbb{H}^{d}. The orbifold M is closed if \Gamma is a cocompact (or uniform), i.e. if its fundamental domain is compact in \mathbb{H}^{d}.

2.2. Thick-thin decomposition

First, we will need some fundamental notions from Riemannian geometry.

It is well known that in any Riemannian manifold (M,g), given a point p\in M and a tangent vector T_{p}M, there is a unique maximal geodesic \gamma_{v}\colon I\to M, where I\subset\mathbb{R} is some interval containing 0, such that \gamma(0)=p and \gamma^{\prime}(0)=v. This allows us to define the exponential map from the tangent bundle TM to M as follows.

Definition 2.1.

Let \mathcal{E}=\{v\in TM\mid\gamma_{v}\text{ is defined on }I\supset[0,1]\}. Then the exponential map \exp:\mathcal{E}\to M is defined by \exp(v)=\gamma_{v}(1).

The exponential map is smooth, its differential at the origin of each T_{p}M is the identity map, whence \exp_{p}\colon\mathcal{E}_{p}\to M, where \mathcal{E}_{p}=\mathcal{E}\cap T_{p}M, is a local diffeomorphism at the origin of T_{p}M. Now we can define the injectivity radius.

Definition 2.2.

Let (M,g) be a Riemannian manifold. The injectivity radius \mathrm{inj}_{p}M of M at a point p\in M is the supremum of all r>0 such that B(0,r)\subset\mathcal{E}_{p} and \exp_{p}\mid_{B(0,r)} is a diffeomorphism onto its image.

Let us now return to the setting of complete hyperbolic manifolds M=\mathbb{H}^{d}/\Gamma, possibly of infinite volume. Since \Gamma acts properly discontinuously by isometries on the universal cover \mathbb{H}^{d} of M, one can reach the minimal distance between the preimages of p\in M in \mathbb{H}^{d}: this minimal distance D is equal to the length of the shortest homotopically non-trivial loop at p. Moreover, \mathrm{inj}_{p}M=D/2. More precisely, denoting by \mathrm{disp}(\gamma) the minimal displacement of \gamma\in\Gamma, we have

\mathrm{inj}_{p}M=\frac{1}{2}\inf\{\mathrm{disp}(\gamma)\mid\gamma\in\Gamma-\{% e\}\}.

Therefore, the injectivity radius of p\in M along a closed simple geodesic of length \varepsilon is precisely \varepsilon/2.

The following group-theoretic result, known as the Margulis Lemma, has broad applications in geometry and topology of hyperbolic manifolds.

Lemma 2.3 (Margulis Lemma).

For every dimension d\geq 2, there is a constant \varepsilon_{d}>0 such that for all x\in\mathbb{H}^{d}, every discrete group \Gamma<\mathrm{Isom}(\mathbb{H}^{d}) generated by elements that move x at distance <\varepsilon_{d} is virtually abelian.

Definition 2.4.

The constant \varepsilon_{d}>0 from Lemma 2.3 is called the Margulis constant.

Definition 2.5 (Thick-Thin Decomposition).

Fix d\geq 2. For any complete hyperbolic d–manifold M and any \varepsilon>0, we define the \varepsilon–thick part

M^{\geq\varepsilon}=\{p\in M\mid\operatorname{inj}_{p}(M)\geq\varepsilon/2\}

and the \varepsilon–thin part

M^{\leq\varepsilon}=\mathrm{clos}(M-M^{\geq\varepsilon}).

Note that if there is a closed geodesic \alpha of length precisely \varepsilon, then \alpha\subset M^{\geq\varepsilon} but \alpha\not\subset M^{\leq\varepsilon}.

The central result on the thick-thin decomposition states (see [27, Theorem 4.2.14]) that if 0<\varepsilon\leq\varepsilon_{d} (the Margulis constant), then the thin part M^{\leq\varepsilon} of any complete hyperbolic d-manifold M is a disjoint union of star-shaped neighbourhoods of cusps and of simple closed geodesics of length <\varepsilon. In fact, all closed geodesics of length <\varepsilon_{d} are simple and disjoint. If d\leq 3 and M is orientable, then the thin part M^{\leq\varepsilon} consists of truncated cusps and R-tubes; see [27, Proposition 4.2.15].

The following fundamental fact (see [27, Proposition 4.2.17 and Corollary 4.2.18] or [4, Proposition D.2.6 and Corollary D.3.14]) about the topology of finite-volume hyperbolic manifolds plays an important role in this paper.

Proposition 2.6.

Let 0<\varepsilon\leq\varepsilon_{d}. A complete hyperbolic d-manifold M has finite volume if and only if its thick part M^{\geq\varepsilon} is compact. Moreover, every complete finite-volume hyperbolic manifold M is diffeomorphic to the interior of a compact manifold with boundary that consists of closed flat manifolds. In particular, any orientable hyperbolic 3-manifold of finite volume can be identified with a compact 3-manifold with boundary consisting of (finitely many) flat tori.

Remark 2.7.

It is known (see [27, Proposition 4.3.1]) that, given a finite-volume hyperbolic manifold M and any number L>0, there are only finitely many closed geodesics in M shorter than L. This allows us to choose \varepsilon to be small enough (namely, smaller than half the systole of M) so that the thin part M^{\leq\varepsilon} consists only of the cusp neighbourhoods and the thick part M^{\geq\varepsilon} is a compact manifold with finitely many flat boundary components, as shows Proposition 2.6. Such a thick part M^{\geq\varepsilon} is homotopy equivalent to M and is thus an aspherical manifold.

2.3. Deformations, convergence, and hyperbolic Dehn filling

2.3.1. Deformations and convergence: general theory

Let G be a (connected) Lie group, and \Gamma an abstract group. Denote by \mathrm{Hom}(\Gamma,G) the set of homomorphisms of the group \Gamma to the group G with the pointwise convergence topology, that is, a sequence \rho_{n}\in\mathrm{Hom}(\Gamma,G) is said to algebraically converge to a homomorphism \rho if \lim_{n\to\infty}\rho_{n}(\gamma)=\rho(\gamma) for every \gamma\in\Gamma.

Let \Gamma_{n} be a sequence of discrete subgroups of \mathrm{Isom}(\mathbb{H}^{d}). We say that \Gamma_{n} converge in the Chabauty topology to \Gamma if the groups converge in the Chabauty topology on closed subsets of G. Convergence in this topology can be characterized as follows:

  • •

    every \gamma\in\Gamma is the limit of some sequence \{\gamma_{n}\} with \gamma_{n}\in\Gamma_{n};

  • •

    if \gamma_{n}\to\gamma is a convergent sequence with \gamma_{n}\in\Gamma_{n}, then \gamma\in\Gamma.

Let \mathscr{H}_{d} denote the set of complete hyperbolic manifolds \mathbb{H}^{d}/\Gamma, with \Gamma<\mathrm{Isom}(\mathbb{H}^{d}) a discrete subgroup, and \mathscr{F}_{d}\subset\mathscr{H}_{d} the subset of finite-volume manifolds (i.e. with \Gamma being a lattice). We equip \mathscr{H}_{d} with the topology of geometric convergence defined as the Chabauty topology on \mathrm{Isom}(\mathbb{H}^{d}). That is, hyperbolic manifolds M_{n}=\mathbb{H}^{d}/\Gamma_{n} converge to M=\mathbb{H}^{d}/\Gamma geometrically if \Gamma_{n}\to\Gamma in the Chabauty topology.

We will need another notion of convergence which happens to be equivalent to the geometric one in the setting of hyperbolic manifolds. For a manifold X, let \mathcal{OF}(X) denote the orthogonal frame bundle of X. Let (M_{1},v_{1}) and (M_{2},v_{2}) be complete hyperbolic d–manifolds with orthonormal baseframes v_{1}\in\mathcal{OF}(M_{1}), v_{2}\in\mathcal{OF}(M_{2}) based at points x_{1}\in M_{1}, x_{2}\in M_{2}. We say that a map f:(X_{1},v_{1})\to(X_{2},v_{2}) is a framed (K,r)–approximate isometry between (M_{1},v_{1}) and (M_{2},v_{2}) if:

  • •

    f:X_{1}\to X_{2} is a diffeomorphism of domains X_{j}\subset M_{j};

  • •

    B_{M_{1}}(x_{1},r)\subset(X_{1},x_{1})\subset(M_{1},x_{1}) and B_{M_{2}}(x_{2},r)\subset(X_{2},x_{2})\subset(M_{2},x_{2});

  • •

    Df(v_{1})=v_{2} (the baseframe is preserved);

  • •

    for all x,y\in X_{1},

    \frac{1}{K}\,d_{M_{1}}(x,y)\;\leq\;d_{M_{2}}\bigl{(}f(x),f(y)\bigr{)}\;\leq\;K% \,d_{M_{1}}(x,y).

Suppose now that M_{i}=\mathbb{H}^{d}/\Gamma_{i} is a sequence of hyperbolic manifolds with baseframes v_{i}\in\mathcal{OF}(M_{i}) based at points x_{i}\in M_{i}, and let M=\mathbb{H}^{d}/\Gamma be a hyperbolic manifold with baseframe v\in\mathcal{OF}(M) based at x\in M. The sequence (M_{i},v_{i}) converges to (M,v) in the induced refined Gromov–Hausdorff topology if if there exist (K_{i},r_{i})–approximate isometries f_{i}:(M_{i},v_{i})\to(M,v) with K_{i}\to 1 and r_{i}\to\infty as i\to\infty.

The following theorem (see Benedetti–Petronio [4, Theorem E.1.13 and Remark E.1.19] and Canary–Epstein–Green [8, Theorem 3.2.9 and Corollary 3.2.11]) shows that the geometric convergence is equivalent to the classical pointed Gromov–Hausdorff convergence and the stronger convergence in the refined Gromov–Hausdorff topology, as well as to the pointed Gromov–Hausdorff convergence of the corresponding Dirichlet fundamental polyhedra.

Theorem 2.8.

Let d\geq 2. The following are equivalent.

  1. (1)

    A sequence of torsion-free discrete subgroups \Gamma_{n}<\mathrm{Isom}(\mathbb{H}^{d}) geometrically converges to a torsion-free discrete subgroup \Gamma<\mathrm{Isom}(\mathbb{H}^{d}).

  2. (2)

    The pointed hyperbolic manifolds (\mathbb{H}^{d}/\Gamma_{n},v_{n}) converge to (\mathbb{H}^{d}/\Gamma,v) in the refined Gromov–Hausdorff topology for some choice of orthonormal baseframes v_{n}\in\mathcal{OF}(\mathbb{H}^{d}/\Gamma_{n}) and v\in\mathcal{OF}(\mathbb{H}^{d}/\Gamma).

  3. (3)

    For a fixed origin o\in\mathbb{H}^{d}, the Dirichlet fundamental polyhedra D_{\Gamma_{n}}(o) converge in the pointed Gromov–Hausdorff topology to the Dirichlet fundamental polyhedron D_{\Gamma}(o). The corresponding side-pairing generators of \Gamma_{n} also converge to that of \Gamma.

Corollary 2.8.1.

If \Gamma_{n}\to\Gamma geometrically, then for any compact subset K\subset M=\mathbb{H}^{d}/\Gamma there is N>0 such that for any n>N there exists a (1+\varepsilon_{n})-bi-Lipschitz embedding \phi_{n}:K\hookrightarrow M_{n}=\mathbb{H}^{d}/\Gamma_{n}, which is a diffeomorphism onto its image \phi_{n}(K).

Proof.

Suppose that K\subset M is obtained from a compact lift \widetilde{K}\subset\mathbb{H}^{d} by projection \mathbb{H}^{d}\to\mathbb{H}^{d}/\Gamma. One can pick such \widetilde{K} in the Dirichlet fundamental polyhedron D_{\Gamma}(o) for some point o\in\mathbb{H}^{d}. Then there is R>0 such that \widetilde{K}\subset B_{\mathbb{H}^{d}}(o,R). Theorem 2.8 implies that there exist (K_{n},r_{n})–approximate isometries f_{n}:(M_{n},v_{n})\to(M,v) with K_{n}\to 1 and r_{n}\to\infty as n\to\infty. This means that there is N>0 such that for any n>N we have r_{n}>R and a diffeomorphism of domains in M_{n} and M, where the selected domain in M covers the ball B_{M}(x,r_{n}). \scriptstyle\blacksquare

2.3.2. Hyperbolic Dehn filling

Let M be a 3-manifold with the (connected) torus boundary T=\partial M.

Definition 2.9 (Dehn filling).

A Dehn filling of M along T is the operation of gluing a solid torus D\times S^{1} to M via a diffeomorphism \varphi:\partial D\times S^{1}\to T.

The closed curve \partial D\times\{x\} is glued to some simple closed curve \gamma\subset T. The result of this operation is a closed manifold M^{\prime}. It is well known (see, for instance, [27, Proposition 10.1.3]) that

\pi_{1}(M^{\prime})=\pi_{1}(M)/\langle\!\langle\gamma\rangle\!\rangle,

where \langle\!\langle\gamma\rangle\!\rangle denotes the normal closure of \gamma in \pi_{1}(M). This gives a surjective homomorphism f:\pi_{1}(M)\to\pi_{1}(M^{\prime}) with kernel \ker f=\langle\!\langle\gamma\rangle\!\rangle.

We will say that our Dehn filling kills the curve \gamma. Fixing a basis \alpha,\beta for \pi_{1}(T)<\Gamma, we can write isotopy classes (called slopes) of homotopically non-trivial unoriented simple closed curves on T as [\gamma]=\alpha^{p}\beta^{q} for some coprime pair (p,q). This gives a 1-1 correspondence between slopes and elements of \mathbb{Q}\cup\{\infty\}. Therefore, every number \frac{p}{q} corresponds to a Dehn filling that kills an associated slope [\gamma].

Now we need to understand what happens with hyperbolic structures when we perform a Dehn filling procedure. Recall that by Proposition 2.6 any oriented finite-volume hyperbolic 3-manifold M=\mathbb{H}^{3}/\Gamma with one cusp is diffeomorphic to a 3-manifold with the torus boundary. The following theorem shows that almost all Dehn fillings M^{\prime} obtained from M admit complete hyperbolic structures and therefore give rise to closed hyperbolic 3-manifolds.

Theorem 2.10 (Thurston’s hyperbolic Dehn filling).

Let M be a finite-volume hyperbolic 3-manifold with one cusp. Let \mathscr{M} be the set of all 3-manifolds obtained from M via Dehn filling. Then all but finitely many elements of \mathscr{M} are closed hyperbolic 3-manifolds, and \mathscr{M} contains sequences \{M_{n}\} converging to M.

Proof.

For a complete proof of the first part of the theorem we refer to Martelli [27, Theorem 15.1.1 and Corollary 15.1.3]. The second (convergence) part can be found in Benedetti–Petronio [4, Theorem E.5.1]. \scriptstyle\blacksquare

Remark 2.11.

Denote by \rho\colon\Gamma\to\mathrm{Isom}(\mathbb{H}^{3})\cong\mathrm{PSL}_{2}(\mathbb{% C}) the initial embedding of \pi_{1}(M)=\Gamma in \mathrm{PSL}_{2}(\mathbb{C}). Then the above discussed homomorphism f_{p,q}=f:\pi_{1}(M)\to\pi_{1}(M^{\prime}) leads to a deformation of the representation \rho into a (non-faithful) representation \rho_{p,q}:\Gamma\to\mathrm{PSL}_{2}(\mathbb{C}), where \rho_{p,q}=\rho^{\prime}\circ f with \rho^{\prime}:\pi_{1}(M^{\prime})\to\mathrm{PSL}_{2}(\mathbb{C}) being a faithful representation of \pi_{1}(M^{\prime}) obtained in Theorem 2.10.

2.4. Hyperbolic Dehn filling of orientable hyperbolic 3-orbifolds

In the previous subsection, we discussed the hyperbolic Dehn filling for manifolds, however, this theory can be adapted to the broader setting of hyperbolic 3-orbifolds, which was done by Dunbar and Meyerhoff [10].

Let \mathcal{O} be a complete, finite-volume, orientable hyperbolic 3-orbifold with at least one cusp. Each cusp admits a horospherical cross-section which is a closed Euclidean 2-orbifold \mathcal{F}. Thus, \mathcal{F} is either a nonsingular torus, a pillowcase (see Figure 1), or a turnover of type S^{2}(n_{1},n_{2},n_{3}) with \frac{1}{n_{1}}+\frac{1}{n_{2}}+\frac{1}{n_{3}}=1:

\mathbb{T}^{2},\quad S^{2}(2,2,2,2),\quad S^{2}(3,3,3),\quad S^{2}(2,3,6),% \quad S^{2}(2,4,4).

We recall that S^{2}(a_{1},\dots,a_{k}) denotes an orbifold with underlying topological space \mathbb{S}^{2} and cone points of orders a_{1},\dots,a_{k}.

Let \Gamma=\pi_{1}^{\mathrm{orb}}(\mathcal{O}), and let \Gamma_{u}<\Gamma be a maximal parabolic subgroup corresponding to a cusp; \Gamma_{u} is precisely the stabilizer of a point u\in\partial\mathbb{H}^{3}. The cusp cross-section \mathcal{F} is naturally identified with the Euclidean orbifold \mathbb{E}^{2}/\Gamma_{u}, and \Gamma_{u} fits into a short exact sequence

1\longrightarrow\Lambda\cong\mathbb{Z}^{2}\longrightarrow\Gamma_{u}% \longrightarrow H\longrightarrow 1,

where \Lambda is the subgroup of translations in \Gamma_{u} and H is a finite subgroup of \mathrm{O}_{2}(\mathbb{R}), the rotational holonomy of \mathcal{F}.

A cusp of \mathcal{O} is called non-rigid (or flexible) if its cross-section is \mathbb{T}^{2} or the pillowcase S^{2}(2,2,2,2), and rigid if its cross-section is S^{2}(3,3,3), S^{2}(2,3,6) or S^{2}(2,4,4). Equivalently, F is non-rigid if and only if it admits a nontrivial 1-parameter family of Euclidean similarity structures, i.e. if its Teichmüller space has positive dimension, and rigid otherwise.

For a non-rigid cusp, the subgroup of translations \Lambda\cong\mathbb{Z}^{2} in \Gamma_{p} is well defined up to conjugacy. A slope on the cusp is, by definition, an unoriented isotopy class of essential simple closed curves on F in the orbifold sense. Group-theoretically, a slope determines a primitive element \lambda\in\Lambda, well defined up to inversion and the action of the finite holonomy H. One can therefore regard the set of slopes as the set of H-orbits of primitive elements in \Lambda modulo \lambda\sim\lambda^{-1}.

Topological Dehn filling on a torus cusp is just the usual operation from the manifold case: one glues a solid torus along the boundary component, identifying a chosen slope on \mathbb{T}^{2} with a meridian of the solid torus. In the orbifold setting one may more generally perform orbifold Dehn filling by gluing a solid toric orbifold whose core curve is a cone geodesic of order m\in\mathbb{N}; the case m=1 recovers manifold Dehn filling.

For a cusp with cross-section S^{2}(2,2,2,2), there is an analogous operation where one glues in a “solid pillow,” i.e. a 3-ball with two unknotted singular arcs labelled by \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}. This construction can be regarded as the quotient of the torus case by the elliptic involution on a 2-fold torus cover of the cusp. In this setting, a slope is represented by a simple closed curve on S^{2}(2,2,2,2) which separates the four order-2 cone points into two pairs, and Dehn filling identifies this curve with a meridian of the solid pillow.

In contrast, there is no nontrivial Dehn filling operation on a rigid cusp: no quotient of a solid torus has a Euclidean turnover as boundary, so one cannot topologically cap off a turnover boundary component by a solid toric orbifold. Thus, Dehn filling is only defined on cusps whose cross-sections are \mathbb{T}^{2} or S^{2}(2,2,2,2).

Fix a non-rigid cusp of \mathcal{O} with peripheral subgroup \Gamma_{u}<\Gamma and translation subgroup \Lambda\cong\mathbb{Z}^{2}. Let \lambda\in\Lambda be a primitive element representing a slope on the cusp in the sense above.

  • •

    If the cusp cross-section is a torus, \mathcal{F}=\mathbb{T}^{2}, then \Gamma_{u}=\Lambda\cong\mathbb{Z}^{2} and we may choose generators a,b for \Gamma_{u} so that \lambda=a^{p}b^{q} for some coprime integers p,q.

  • •

    If the cross-section is the pillowcase \mathcal{F}=S^{2}(2,2,2,2), then we have the short exact sequence

    1\longrightarrow\Lambda\cong\mathbb{Z}^{2}\longrightarrow\Gamma_{u}% \longrightarrow\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}\longrightarrow 1,

    and one may choose a presentation

    \Gamma_{u}=\langle a,b,t\mid[a,b]=1,\ t^{2}=1,\ tat^{-1}=a^{-1},\ tbt^{-1}=b^{% -1}\rangle,

    where \langle a,b\rangle\cong\mathbb{Z}^{2} is the subgroup of translations and t is the elliptic involution. A slope is again represented by a primitive element \lambda=a^{p}b^{q}\in\Lambda.

Given \lambda and an integer m\geq 1, the orbifold Dehn filling of slope \lambda and cone order m is, at the level of orbifold fundamental groups, the quotient

\Gamma(\lambda;m):=\Gamma\big{/}\langle\!\langle\lambda^{m}\rangle\!\rangle,

where \langle\!\langle\cdot\rangle\!\rangle denotes normal closure in \Gamma. When \mathcal{F}=\mathbb{T}^{2} and m=1, this recovers the usual manifold Dehn filling, and for general m it corresponds to turning the core curve of the attached solid torus into a cone geodesic of order m. When \mathcal{F}=S^{2}(2,2,2,2) this describes the case of gluing a solid pillow whose core circle has cone order m.

The analytic and geometric theory of Dehn filling extends to orbifolds almost verbatim from the manifold case. Fix a finite-volume orientable hyperbolic 3-orbifold \mathcal{O} with at least one non-rigid cusp. By the hyperbolic Dehn surgery theorem (see Dunbar–Meyerhoff [10, Theorem 5.3]) there exists a finite set of slopes \mathcal{E} on the non-rigid cusps of \mathcal{O} such that the following holds. For each choice of slopes \lambda_{i} on the non-rigid cusps of \mathcal{O} with \lambda_{i}\notin\mathcal{E}, and each choice of sufficiently large cone orders m_{i}\in\mathbb{N}, the orbifold \mathcal{O}_{\lambda_{i};m_{i}} obtained by topological orbifold Dehn filling along the (\lambda_{i};m_{i}) admits a unique complete hyperbolic orbifold structure. Moreover, its orbifold fundamental group is naturally isomorphic to the quotient of \Gamma obtained by adding the relations \lambda_{i}^{m_{i}}=1, and as the lengths of the filling slopes (and the cone orders m_{i}) tend to infinity, the resulting hyperbolic orbifolds \mathcal{O}_{\lambda_{i};m_{i}} converge geometrically to \mathcal{O}, see Dunbar–Meyerhoff [10, Theorems 5.4 and 6.4].

In particular, every closed orientable hyperbolic 3-orbifold arises from a cusped orientable hyperbolic 3-orbifold by a (possibly multi-cusped) orbifold Dehn filling on non-rigid cusps, exactly mirroring the manifold setting; see [10, Theorem 5.5].

2.5. Hyperbolic Dehn filling in the case of hyperbolic reflection groups

Generally, in the case of non-orientable manifolds or orbifolds, one can simply pass to the orientable double cover and then discuss the Dehn filling process and geometric convergence in the oriented case. However, for discrete groups \Gamma generated by reflections in hyperplanes, the Dehn filling process deserves to be considered separately since it can be described more explicitly in terms of the corresponding Coxeter polyhedra. It turns out that the Dehn fillings \Gamma_{n} obtained from \Gamma can also be forced to be reflection groups, whose fundamental Coxeter polyhedra P_{n} converge to the polyhedron P by contracting a certain finite edge.

Let Q\subset\mathbb{H}^{3} be a finite-volume Coxeter polyhedron with an edge e with two finite vertices, and let the dihedral angle at e be \frac{\pi}{m} for some m\geq 6. Note that since m\geq 6, the dihedral angle at each edge adjacent to e in Q is \frac{\pi}{2}. By Andreev’s theorem [1, 2], there always exists a finite-volume Coxeter polyhedron Q^{\prime} of the same combinatorial type and with the same dihedral angles as Q except that the dihedral angle at e in Q^{\prime} is replaced with \frac{\pi}{n}. If n\geq m, we say that Q^{\prime} is obtained from Q via a \frac{\pi}{n}-contraction at e. Thus, we can denote the polyhedra with angle \pi/n at e by P_{n}. Andreev’s theorem also allows to contract this edge e to an ideal vertex v_{\infty}: combinatorially it means that we replace e with a vertex of valence 4 without changing the remaining combinatorics of the polyhedron. This contraction gives rise to a finite-volume Coxeter polyhedron P. We refer to Kolpakov [23] for more details on contractions of edges.

What is important to us here is that we can view P_{n} as having been obtained from P via Dehn filling. Indeed, let v be an ideal vertex of P, and let v be the intersection of facets F_{1},F_{2},F_{3},F_{4} of P; see Figure 2, the middle. These facets intersect any horosphere centered at v in a Euclidean square, and therefore the corresponding reflections r_{1},r_{2},r_{3},r_{4} generate the parabolic reflection group

\Gamma_{v}=\langle r_{1},r_{2},r_{3},r_{4}\mid r_{i}^{2}=(r_{1}r_{2})^{2}=(r_{% 2}r_{3})^{2}=(r_{3}r_{4})^{2}=(r_{1}r_{4})^{2}=e\rangle,

which is isomorphic to (\nicefrac{{\mathbb{Z}}}{{2\mathbb{Z}}}*\nicefrac{{\mathbb{Z}}}{{2\mathbb{Z}}}% )\times(\nicefrac{{\mathbb{Z}}}{{2\mathbb{Z}}}*\nicefrac{{\mathbb{Z}}}{{2% \mathbb{Z}}}).

Note that the parabolic isometries a=r_{1}r_{3} and b=r_{2}r_{4} commute and generate a \mathbb{Z}\times\mathbb{Z} subgroup of \Gamma_{v}. Algebraically, the (p,q)-Dehn filling in this case means the quotient by the normal closure of an isometry a^{p}b^{q}. Generally, it gives non-Coxeter groups because of the new relation (r_{1}r_{3})^{p}(r_{2}r_{4})^{q}=e. However, in the case of (n,0)- or (0,n)-Dehn fillings we obtain precisely the Coxeter groups \Gamma_{n} or \Gamma^{\prime}_{n} generated by reflections in the walls of P_{n} or P^{\prime}_{n}, respectively, as in Figure 2. Indeed, this gives only an extra relation (r_{1}r_{3})^{n}=e or (r_{2}r_{4})^{n}=e in the quotient, therefore giving a Coxeter group. Denoting the generators in the quotient by r_{i,n} or r^{\prime}_{i,n} in the case of (n,0)- and (0,n)-Dehn fillings, we obtain precisely the Coxeter groups \Gamma_{n} and \Gamma^{\prime}_{n}, generated by reflections in the walls of Coxeter polyhedra P_{n} and P^{\prime}_{n}. Combinatorially, these two sequences of polyhedra P_{n} and P^{\prime}_{n} are obtained from the polyhedron P by replacing a vertex v with a compact edge in only two possible ways, as shown in Figure 2.

As in the case of general Dehn fillings, such contractions of edges to infinity do not exist in higher dimensions (it was explicitly shown in [7, Theorem 4.2] for almost right-angled polyhedra).

2.6. Random walks

Let \Gamma be a countable discrete group acting properly discontinuously on a metric space (X,d) by isometries, and let \mu be a probability measure on \Gamma. Consider a random walk

\omega_{n}:=g_{1}\dots g_{n},

where the (g_{i}) are i.i.d. with distribution \mu. We will say that a random walk (\omega_{n}) has finite range if \mu has finite support, and we will call it non-degenerate if the support of \mu generates \Gamma as a semigroup.

Let o\in X be a basepoint. Let \mu^{*n}=\underbrace{\mu*\ldots*\mu}_{n\text{ times}} be the nth convolution of the measure \mu with itself. Then one defines:

  1. (1)

    the asymptotic entropy h(\mu), introduced by Avez [3],

    h(\mu):=\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{H(\mu^{*n})}{n}:=\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{1}{n}% \sum_{g}-\mu^{*n}(g)\log\mu^{*n}(g),

    where H(\cdot) denotes the ordinary entropy defined for any measure \lambda as follows: for any countable set A we have

    H(\lambda):=-\sum_{a\in A}\lambda(a)\log\lambda(a);
  2. (2)

    the drift \ell(\mu) of the random walk

    \ell(\mu):=\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{d(o,\omega_{n}o)}{n}

    where, as long as \mu has finite first moment, the limit exists almost surely and is independent of \omega_{n} and o;

  3. (3)

    the critical exponent v of the action

    v:=\limsup_{R\to\infty}\frac{1}{R}\log\#\{g\in\Gamma\ \mid\ d(o,go)\leq R\}.

These quantities are related by the fundamental inequality due to Guivarc’h [15] (see also Vershik [32], who suggested the name fundamental inequality)

h(\mu)\leq\ell(\mu)v.

Let us denote the support of \mu by S. Recall that any random walk on a non-elementary subgroup of \mathrm{Isom}(\mathbb{H}^{d}) will converge (see Maher–Tiozzo [26]) to the geometric boundary \mathbb{S}^{d-1}=\partial\mathbb{H}^{d}. Equivalently, this means that for \mathbb{P}-a.e. sample path (\omega_{1},\omega_{2},\dots) the limit \lim_{n\rightarrow\infty}\omega_{n}o=\omega_{\infty}\in\mathbb{S}^{d-1} exists, where \mathbb{P} is the probability measure which comes from (S^{\mathbb{N}},\mu^{\otimes\mathbb{N}}) via the pushforward

(g_{1},g_{2},g_{3},\dots)\mapsto(g_{1},g_{1}g_{2},g_{1}g_{2}g_{3},\cdots).

Thus, a random walk induces the (unique) hitting measure \nu_{\mu}=i_{*}(\mathbb{P}), where i is the (\mathbb{P}-a.e. defined) mapping which takes a sample path (\omega_{n})_{n\in\mathbb{N}} to the limit \omega_{\infty}\in S^{2}. Another way to express this definition is to write

Definition 2.12.

For any Borel set A\subseteq\partial\mathbb{H}^{d},

\nu_{\mu}(A)=\mathbb{P}\left(\left\{\lim_{n\to\infty}\omega_{n}o\in A\right\}% \right).

We will be dealing with hyperbolic groups \Gamma acting on \mathbb{H}^{2} and \mathbb{H}^{3}, and in our setting, we can make use of the following powerful criterion. We cite it here in the version for geometrically finite actions from [14, Theorem 4.1], which extends the previous version [5, Theorem 1.5] for hyperbolic groups. In this context, a Patterson-Sullivan measure for the action of \Gamma on a \delta-hyperbolic space X is a quasiconformal measure on \partial X of dimension equal to the critical exponent v of \Gamma.

Theorem 2.13.

Let (X,d) be a \delta-hyperbolic, proper metric space, let \Gamma be a geometrically finite group of isometries of X, and let o\in X be a basepoint.

Let \mu be a non-degenerate measure on \Gamma with finite superexponential moment, let \nu be its corresponding hitting measure, and let \kappa be a Patterson-Sullivan measure on \partial X.

Then the following conditions are equivalent.

  1. (1)

    The equality

    h(\mu)=\ell(\mu)v

    holds.

  2. (2)

    The measures \nu and \kappa are in the same measure class.

  3. (3)

    The measures \nu and \kappa are in the same measure class with Radon–Nikodym derivatives bounded from above and below.

  4. (4)

    There exists C\geq 0 such that for every g\in\Gamma,

    |d_{G}(e,g)-vd(o,go)|\leq C,

    where d_{G} denotes the Green metric for the random walk driven by \mu.

Corollary 2.13.1 ([14], Corollary 4.2).

If \Gamma\curvearrowright X is not convex cocompact then \kappa and \nu are mutually singular.

In this paper, we are interested in X=\mathbb{H}^{d} and \Gamma<\textup{Isom}(\mathbb{H}^{d}) a lattice. In this case it is well known that the Lebesgue measure \lambda on \partial\mathbb{H}^{d} is a Patterson-Sullivan measure.

To see why, recall that if g\in\textup{Isom}(\mathbb{H}^{d}) then (see e.g. [28, Proposition 3.9])

\frac{dg\lambda}{d\lambda}(\xi)=e^{-(d-1)\beta_{\xi}(go,o)}\qquad\forall\xi\in% \partial\mathbb{H}^{d}

so \lambda is quasiconformal and, since \Gamma is a lattice, v=n-1.

The corollary then immediately shows that if \Gamma<\textup{Isom}(\mathbb{H}^{d}) is a nonuniform lattice and \mu an admissible, finitely supported measure on \Gamma, then the hitting measure \nu is singular with respect to Lebesgue, and moreover h(\mu)<(d-1)\ell(\mu).

3.1. Inequality for the entropy

Proposition 3.1.

Let \Gamma be a discrete group, and N\triangleleft\Gamma its normal subgroup. Let \mu_{\Gamma} be a probability measure on \Gamma and \mu_{\Lambda} be the pushforward of \mu_{\Gamma} to \Lambda=\Gamma/N through the homomorphism \varphi:\Gamma\to\Gamma/N. Then h(\mu_{\Lambda})\leq h(\mu_{\Gamma}).

Proof.

First, we note that \mu_{\Lambda}(\gamma N)=\sum_{h\in\gamma N}\mu_{\Gamma}(h). Using the monotonicity of \log, we have -\log\mu_{\Lambda}(\gamma N)=-\log\left(\sum_{h\in\gamma N}\mu_{\Gamma}(h)% \right)\leq-\log\mu_{\Gamma}(h) and therefore

\begin{split}-\mu_{\Lambda}(\gamma N)\log\mu_{\Lambda}(\gamma N)=&\ -\left(% \sum_{h\in\gamma N}\mu_{\Gamma}(h)\right)\cdot\log\left(\sum_{h\in\gamma N}\mu% _{\Gamma}(h)\right)\\ =&\ -\sum_{h\in\gamma N}\mu_{\Gamma}(h)\log\mu_{\Lambda}(\gamma N)\\ \leq&\ -\sum_{h\in\gamma N}\mu_{\Gamma}(h)\log\mu_{\Gamma}(h).\end{split}

Hence,

\begin{split}H(\mu_{\Lambda})=&\ -\sum_{\lambda\in\Lambda=\Gamma/N}\mu_{% \Lambda}(\lambda)\log\mu_{\Lambda}(\lambda)=-\sum_{\gamma N}\mu_{\Lambda}(% \gamma N)\log\mu_{\Lambda}(\gamma N)\\ \leq&\ -\sum_{\gamma N}\sum_{h\in\gamma N}\mu_{\Gamma}(h)\cdot\log\mu_{\Gamma}% (h)\\ =&-\sum_{g\in\Gamma}\mu_{\Gamma}(g)\cdot\log\left(\mu_{\Gamma}(g)\right)=H(\mu% _{\Gamma}).\end{split}

A very similar argument shows that H(\mu_{\Lambda}^{*n})\leq H(\mu_{\Gamma}^{*n}). Dividing the latter inequality by n and taking the limit \lim_{n\to\infty}, we complete the proof. \scriptstyle\blacksquare

3.2. Algebraic vs geometric convergence of hyperbolic lattices

Here we summarize several specific facts about algebraic and geometric convergence that we will need to prove our main theorems. For some of the claims, we did not find proofs in the literature, so we provide a detailed argument here, although all these facts are known. In particular, part (1) is stated by Kapovich in his book, see [20, Exercise 8.12]. Under the assumption that all \Gamma_{n} are lattices, part (2) follows from [4, Prop. E.2.3] and [4, Prop. E.3.1], and part (3) follows from [4, Theorem E.4.8], as we explain in the proof below.

Theorem 3.2.

Let \Gamma_{n},\Gamma<\mathrm{Isom}(\mathbb{H}^{d}), d\geq 3, be torsion-free discrete groups. Assume that \Gamma_{n}\to\Gamma geometrically and \Gamma is a lattice. Then the following assertions hold.

  1. (1)

    There exists N>0 such that for all n>N the manifolds M_{n}=\mathbb{H}^{d}/\Gamma_{n} have finite volume, i.e. \Gamma_{n} are also lattices.

  2. (2)

    If, moreover, \Gamma is a uniform lattice, or non-uniform but d\geq 4, then \Gamma_{n} are eventually isomorphic to \Gamma, i.e. there is N>0 such that \Gamma_{n}\cong\Gamma for all n>N.

  3. (3)

    If d=3 and \Gamma is non-uniform, then there is N>0 such that for n>N each \Gamma_{n} is obtained from \Gamma by hyperbolic Dehn filling and therefore \Gamma_{n}=\rho_{n}(\Gamma) which algebraically converge to the initial representation \rho(\Gamma)=\Gamma.

Proof.

All parts follow from the thick-thin decomposition and the tools of geometric convergence, but part (1) also requires some standard facts from algebraic topology. By assumption \Gamma is a lattice, and thus the thick part M^{\geq\varepsilon} of M, where \varepsilon\leq\varepsilon_{d}, is compact by Proposition 2.6.

We will first consider the case where \Gamma is a uniform lattice, i.e. where the manifold M=\mathbb{H}^{d}/\Gamma is closed. Taking the compact set K=M, we obtain by Corollary 2.8.1 that there is N>0 such that for all n>N there exist the bi-Lipschitz diffeomorphisms \phi_{n}:M\hookrightarrow M_{n}=\mathbb{H}^{d}/\Gamma_{n}. Then \phi_{n}(M)=N_{n} is a closed d-submanifold of the connected d-manifold M_{n}. This implies that N_{n} is both an open and closed subset of the connected manifold M_{n}, whence N_{n}=M_{n}. Thus, M_{n} itself is a compact hyperbolic d-manifold diffeomorphic to M, and then the Mostow rigidity implies that \Gamma_{n} is isomorphic to \Gamma for all n>N. This proves part (1) and (2) for the case of \Gamma being a uniform lattice.

Henceforth, assume that M=\mathbb{H}^{d}/\Gamma has finite volume and is noncompact, i.e. it has (finitely many) cusps. In this case, we can choose \varepsilon\leq\varepsilon_{d} so that the thick part M^{\geq\varepsilon} is a compact connected manifold whose boundary components are closed flat (d-1)-manifolds F_{i} (see Remark 2.7 based on Proposition 2.6). We will separately treat the cases d=3 and d>3. For simplicity, we can pass simultaneously to orientation preserving subgroups of \Gamma_{n} and \Gamma; so without loss of generality we assume that all manifolds M_{n} and M are orientable.

Consider the compact set K=M^{\geq\varepsilon} and the submanifolds N_{n}=\phi_{n}(K) for the bi-Lipschitz diffeomorphisms \phi_{n}\colon K\hookrightarrow M_{n} (which exist for all n>N for some N>0) from Corollary 2.8.1. The complement M_{n}-N_{n} has connected components C_{i} each having the boundary flat components F^{\prime}_{i}, diffeomorphic to closed flats F_{i}; see Figure 3. One can show that each C_{i} is an aspherical manifold based on the fact that M_{n}, N_{n}, and F^{\prime}_{i} are all aspherical.

Case d=3. In this dimension, the situation is simpler: F_{i} are just flat 2-dimensional tori T_{i}. We will denote their images by T^{\prime}_{i}. Since T^{\prime}_{i} is the boundary of C_{i}, by [27, Proposition 9.3.6] we have only two options.

(a) T^{\prime}_{i} bounds a solid torus, so C_{i} itself is a solid torus and therefore compact.

(b) T^{\prime}_{i} is incompressible in C_{i}, and hence \pi_{1}-injective by Hempel [18, Lemma 6.1]. Since T_{i} is \pi_{1}-injectuve for M, we thus obtain that T^{\prime}_{i} is \pi_{1}-injective for the whole M_{n}. Then T^{\prime}_{i} is a cusp cross-section, since the subgroup \mathbb{Z}\times\mathbb{Z}=\pi_{1}(T^{\prime}_{i}) of \pi_{1}(M_{n})=\Gamma_{n} must preserve a point on the ideal boundary by [27, Corollary 4.2.4]. Hence, the entire C_{i} is just a finite-volume truncated cusp of M_{n}.

Thus, M_{n} consists of the compact block N_{n}=\phi_{n}(M^{\geq\varepsilon}) and of several pieces C_{i} each of them being either compact or finite volume. Then M_{n} has finite volume.

Case d>3. In this case, the flat boundary components of M^{\geq\varepsilon} are some closed (d-1)-manifolds F_{i} that are finitely covered by flat tori T_{i} of rank d-1. These flat manifolds F_{i} are embedded \pi_{1}-injectively in M, and, as in the case d=3, we again analyze their images F^{\prime}_{i}=\phi_{n}(F_{i}) in M_{n}.

We again have two options.

(a) F^{\prime}_{i} is not \pi_{1}-injective in C_{i}. Then C_{i} is compact as follows from the following two lemmas.

Lemma 3.3.

Let X be a connected aspherical d-manifold and F\subset X a closed, connected, embedded flat hypersurface. Assume that the inclusion i\colon F\hookrightarrow X is not \pi_{1}-injective. Then i_{*}([F]) is a torsion element of H_{d-1}(X;\mathbb{Z}). In particular, the image of [F] in H_{d-1}(X;\mathbb{Q}) is zero.

Proof.

We write \Gamma=\pi_{1}(X). Since X is aspherical, it is a K(\Gamma,1)-space and \Gamma is torsion-free. By the Bieberbach theorem, F is finitely covered by a torus \mathbb{T}^{d-1}.

Case 1: F is a torus \mathbb{T}^{d-1}. Then i_{*}:\pi_{1}(\mathbb{T}^{d-1})\cong\mathbb{Z}^{d-1}\to\Gamma has nontrivial kernel by assumption, so its image is a free abelian group \mathbb{Z}^{k} with k<d-1.

Since \mathbb{T}^{d-1} and X are Eilenberg–MacLane spaces K(\mathbb{Z}^{d-1},1) and K(\Gamma,1), the map i is, up to homotopy, determined by i_{*} and factors through the classifying space K(\mathbb{Z}^{k},1)\cong\mathbb{T}^{k}. Thus there exist maps

\mathbb{T}^{d-1}\xrightarrow{g}\mathbb{T}^{k}\xrightarrow{f}X

inducing the homomorphisms \mathbb{Z}^{d-1}\twoheadrightarrow\mathbb{Z}^{k}\to\Gamma on \pi_{1}, such that i is homotopic to f\circ g.

On homology with \mathbb{Z}-coefficients we have H_{d-1}(\mathbb{T}^{d-1};\mathbb{Z})\cong\mathbb{Z}, generated by the fundamental class [\mathbb{T}^{d-1}], while H_{d-1}(\mathbb{T}^{k};\mathbb{Z})=0 since k<d-1. Hence g_{*}[\mathbb{T}^{d-1}]=0\in H_{d-1}(\mathbb{T}^{k};\mathbb{Z}), and therefore

i_{*}[\mathbb{T}^{d-1}]=f_{*}g_{*}[\mathbb{T}^{d-1}]=0\in H_{d-1}(X;\mathbb{Z}).

Case 2: F is only virtually a torus. In this case, F is a closed flat (d-1)-manifold with a finite covering p:\mathbb{T}^{d-1}\longrightarrow F. The covering p induces a short exact sequence

1\longrightarrow\pi_{1}(\mathbb{T}^{d-1})\cong\mathbb{Z}^{d-1}\longrightarrow% \pi_{1}(F)\longrightarrow A\longrightarrow 1,

where A is finite and \pi_{1}(F) is a Bieberbach group, hence torsion-free.

Let \varphi=i_{*}:\pi_{1}(F)\to\pi_{1}(X). By assumption \varphi is not injective, so

K:=\ker(\varphi)\subset\pi_{1}(F)

is nontrivial. Suppose first that K\cap\pi_{1}(\mathbb{T}^{d-1}) is trivial. Then the restriction of the projection \pi_{1}(F)\to A to K is injective, so K embeds into the finite group A and is therefore finite. But \pi_{1}(F) is torsion-free, so it cannot contain a nontrivial finite subgroup. This contradiction shows that K\cap\pi_{1}(\mathbb{T}^{d-1}) is non-trivial.

Thus, the composition

\pi_{1}(\mathbb{T}^{d-1})\xrightarrow{p_{*}}\pi_{1}(F)\xrightarrow{i_{*}}\pi_{% 1}(X)

has nontrivial kernel, i.e. the induced map i\circ p:\mathbb{T}^{d-1}\to X is not \pi_{1}-injective. By Case 1, the induced map on (d-1)-dimensional homology satisfies

(i\circ p)_{*}[\mathbb{T}^{d-1}]=0\in H_{d-1}(X;\mathbb{Z}).

On the other hand, since p is a finite covering of degree m>0, we have

p_{*}[\mathbb{T}^{d-1}]=m\,[F]\in H_{d-1}(F;\mathbb{Z}).

Therefore

0=(i\circ p)_{*}[\mathbb{T}^{d-1}]=i_{*}p_{*}[\mathbb{T}^{d-1}]=i_{*}\bigl{(}m% \,[F]\bigr{)}=m\,i_{*}[F]\in H_{d-1}(X;\mathbb{Z}).

Hence i_{*}[F] is a torsion element of H_{d-1}(X;\mathbb{Z}). In particular, its image in H_{d-1}(X;\mathbb{Q}) is zero. \scriptstyle\blacksquare

Lemma 3.4.

If F=\partial X is the compact connected boundary of a connected d-manifold X and [F]=0 in the H_{d-1}(X), then X is compact.

Proof.

We can work over the field \mathbb{Z}_{2}=\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z} or over the field \mathbb{Q} if X is oriented. Consider the double DX of X along the boundary. Applying the Mayer–Vietoris to the classical decomposition DX=A\cup B, where A\cap B=F\times(-1,1)\simeq F, and A and B are thickenings of X by the collars F\times(-1,0] and F\times[0,1), respectively, we obtain that [F]\in\mathrm{im}(\delta) where \delta:H_{d}(DX)\to H_{d-1}(F). Indeed, since F has dimension d-1, H_{d}(F)=0, so the relevant part of the Mayer–Vietoris sequence is

0\to H_{d}(A)\oplus H_{d}(B)\longrightarrow H_{d}(DX)\longrightarrow H_{d-1}(F% )\xrightarrow{j_{*}}H_{d-1}(A)\oplus H_{d-1}(B).

Here j_{*} is induced by the inclusions F\hookrightarrow A and F\hookrightarrow B. Identifying A and B with X, we can write

j_{*}=(i_{*},-i_{*}):H_{d-1}(F)\longrightarrow H_{d-1}(X)\oplus H_{d-1}(X),

where i_{*}:H_{d-1}(F)\to H_{d-1}(X) is the map induced by the inclusion i:F\hookrightarrow X. By assumption, i_{*}[F]=0 in H_{d-1}(X), so

j_{*}[F]=(i_{*}[F],-i_{*}[F])=(0,0).

Thus [F]\in\ker(j_{*}). By exactness, \ker(j_{*}) is the image of \delta, whence [F]\in\mathrm{im}(\delta). On the other hand, [F]\neq 0 in H_{d-1}(F) and thus H_{d}(DX)\neq 0.

Now DX is a connected d-manifold without boundary and has nonzero top-dimensional homology H_{d}(DX). It is a standard fact that a connected noncompact d-manifold without boundary has H_{d}=0 (see, for example, Hatcher [17, Proposition 3.29]). Therefore DX must be compact. \scriptstyle\blacksquare

This proves part (1).

Part (2) follows from [4, Prop. E.2.3] and [4, Prop. E.3.1].

Part (3) follows from [4, Theorem E.4.8] which states the following. Recall that \mathcal{F} is the set of all complete finite-volume hyperbolic 3-manifolds with the topology of geometric convergence. Let \mathscr{F}_{3}(c)\subset\mathcal{F} be the subset of manifolds M with \operatorname{Vol}M\leq c. Then there exists a finite collection of finite-volume manifolds X_{1},\ldots,X_{k} with cusps such that X\in\mathscr{F}_{3}(c) is obtained from some X_{i} by hyperbolic Dehn surgery.

In our case, we have M_{n}=\mathbb{H}^{3}/\Gamma_{n} geometrically converge to M=\mathbb{H}^{3}/\Gamma, whence, for n large enough, the volumes \operatorname{Vol}M_{n} are bounded from above and thus we can apply [4, Theorem E.4.8]. \scriptstyle\blacksquare

3.3. Compatible measures on convergent sequences of lattices

Definition 3.5.

Assume that \Gamma_{n}=\rho_{n}(\Gamma) algebraically converge to \Gamma=\rho_{\infty}(\Gamma). Measures \mu_{n} on \Gamma_{n} are said to be compatible if, for all n, we have \mu_{n}=(\rho_{n})_{*}\mu of some measure \mu on \Gamma.

Lemma 3.6.

Let \{\Gamma_{n}\}_{n=1}^{\infty} be a sequence of pairwise non-conjugate lattices in \mathrm{Isom}(\mathbb{H}^{3}) geometrically converging to a lattice \Gamma. Then there is N>0 such that for any measure \mu on \Gamma, the remaining tail \{\Gamma_{n}\}^{\infty}_{n=N+1} of this sequence can be equipped with compatible measures \mu_{n}. Moreover, if for some m>N, the lattice \Gamma_{m} is equipped with a measure \mu_{m} compatible with some measure \mu on \Gamma, then we can equip all the other members of the subsequence \{\Gamma_{n}\}^{\infty}_{n=N+1} with measures \mu_{n} compatible with \mu_{m}.

Proof.

First of all, note that Theorem 3.2 shows that there is N>0 such that for n>N each \Gamma_{n} is obtained from \Gamma by hyperbolic Dehn surgery and therefore \Gamma_{n}=\rho_{n}(\Gamma) converge algebraically to the representation \rho_{\infty}(\Gamma)=\Gamma. Then we set \mu_{n}:=(\rho_{n})_{*}\mu, which proves the first part. For the second part, we note that \mu=(\rho_{m})^{*}\mu_{m} on \Gamma, and then the first part applies. \scriptstyle\blacksquare

4. Proof of main theorems

4.1. Kleinian groups and proof of Theorem 1.2

Let \Gamma_{n}<\mathrm{Isom}(\mathbb{H}^{3}) be an infinite sequence of lattices, geometrically converging to a lattice \Gamma. The assumption of \Gamma_{n} being pairwise non-conjugate implies, by Theorem 3.2, that \Gamma is non-uniform and all but finitely many \Gamma_{n} are obtained from \Gamma by hyperbolic Dehn surgery. Hence, removing finitely many exceptions, we may assume that \Gamma_{n}=\rho_{n}(\Gamma) algebraically converge to \Gamma=\rho_{\infty}(\Gamma). Lemma 3.6 shows that we can equip our subsequence by compatible measures, and this can be done for an arbitrary given measure \mu_{m} on any \Gamma_{m}.

From now on, we assume that the entire sequence \Gamma_{n} is equipped with compatible measures \mu_{n} obtained via pushforward from some measure \mu on \Gamma. Let us denote by h_{n} the entropy h(\mu_{n}), and set \ell_{n}=\ell(\mu_{n}), \ell=\ell(\mu). The hitting measures driven by \mu_{n} and \mu will be denoted by \nu_{n} and \nu, respectively.

Using Proposition 3.1 and the fact that \Gamma_{n}=\rho_{n}(\Gamma)=\Gamma/\ker\rho_{n}, we obtain that h_{n}\leq h. From Theorem 2.13 we know that \nu_{n}\perp\lambda if and only if h_{n}<\ell_{n}v_{n}. Here v_{n}=v=2 for all n. The drift is continuous for random walks on subgroups of \mathrm{GL}_{2}(\mathbb{C}) (that is, the continuity of the drift applies to finitely subgroups of both \mathrm{PSL}_{2}(\mathbb{C}) and \mathrm{PSL}_{2}(\mathbb{R})), as shown by the recent result of Bocker-Neto and Viana [6], whence we have that \ell_{n}\to\ell as n\to\infty. Finally, Theorem 2.13 (see also Corollary 2.13.1 and the discussion after it) implies that h<2\ell, since the lattice \Gamma<\mathrm{Isom}(\mathbb{H}^{3}) is non-uniform. Therefore,

2\ell_{n}\xrightarrow{n\to\infty}2\ell>h\geq h_{n}.

Hence, there is N>0 such that 2\ell_{n}>h_{n} for all n>N, so \nu_{\mu_{n}} is singular for these values of n. \scriptstyle\blacksquare

4.2. Hyperbolic reflection groups and proof of Theorem 1.4

In this case, the proof is a simple (special) version of the proof Theorem 1.2. Indeed, if the edge e in Coxeter polyhedra P_{n} is formed by facets corresponding to reflections r_{1}^{(n)} and r_{2}^{(n)}, our reflection groups satisfy: \Gamma_{n}=\Gamma/N_{n}, where N_{n} is the normal closure of (r_{1}r_{2})^{n}\in\Gamma. By Proposition 3.1, we have the inequality h_{n}<h for all n. The conclusion of the proof remains the same as in Theorem 1.2. \scriptstyle\blacksquare

4.3. Fuchsian groups and proof of Theorem 1.5

For Fuchsian groups, the result is stated already in the setting of algebraic convergence. This allows to apply Proposition 3.1 directly and then again conclude in the same way as it was done in the proof of Theorem 1.2. \scriptstyle\blacksquare

5. Examples

Throughout this section, \Sigma_{g,a,b} denotes an orientable surface of genus g\geq 1 with a punctures and b boundary components; if b=0, we will simply write \Sigma_{g,a}, and \Sigma_{g} denotes a closed genus-g surface.

5.1. Geometric convergence of infinite-covolume Fuchsian groups to a lattice

Let \Sigma denote an infinite-area surface of genus 1 with one funnel, i.e. infinite end, and let \alpha be a simple closed geodesic separating the funnel, see Figure 4. Cutting off the funnel along \alpha would give us a surface \Sigma_{1,0,1} in the above notation.

To construct a geometrically converging sequence of infinite-covolume Fuchsian groups, we take a surface \Sigma_{n} with the same topology as \Sigma, but replace \alpha with a closed curve \alpha_{n} such that the length of \alpha_{n} tends to 0 as n\to\infty. For sufficiently large n and an appropriate choice of \varepsilon>0, the thin part (\Sigma_{n})^{\leq\varepsilon} of \Sigma_{n} is the neighborhood of \alpha_{n} (bounded by blue arcs, see the top of Figure 4). Then the thick part (\Sigma_{n})^{\geq\varepsilon} has two connected components, one of them being the surface \Sigma_{1,0,1}. Denote this component by T_{n}. Taking the basepoints x_{n}\in T_{n} so that they “stay apart” (uniformly) from the geodesic \alpha_{n}, we obtain the pointed Gromov–Hausdorff convergence of (\Sigma_{n},x_{n}) to (\Sigma_{1,1},x) for some x\in{\Sigma_{1,1}}. By Theorem 2.8, this is equivalent to the geometric convergence of \pi_{1}(\Sigma_{n}) to \pi_{1}(\Sigma_{1,1}), which acts as a lattice on \mathbb{H}^{2}. We note that the groups \pi_{1}(\Sigma_{n}) and \pi_{1}(\Sigma_{1,1}) are all abstractly isomorphic to \mathrm{F}_{2}, a free group of rank 2.

This geometric convergence can also be justified in the setting of the pointed Gromov–Hausdorff convergence of the corresponding Dirichlet fundamental polyhedra. Consider the Poincare disc model of \mathbb{H}^{2} with o the origin. We choose a metric on \Sigma_{1,1} such that the Dirichlet fundamental domain D_{\Gamma}(o) of \Gamma=\pi_{1}(\Sigma_{1,1}) is the regular ideal quadrilateral shown in Figure 5 (right). Then we take another quadrilateral bounded by four pairwise disjoint geodesics such that all the common perpendiculars \alpha_{n} between the adjacent (consecutive) sides have the same length, see Figure 5 (left). These red perpendiculars \alpha_{n} project under the \Gamma_{n}=\pi_{1}(\Sigma_{n})-action precisely to the red geodesic \alpha_{n} illustrated in Figure 4 (top). Shrinking simultaneously all these \alpha_{n}, we obtain the pointed Gromov–Hausdorff convergence D_{\Gamma_{n}}(o)\xrightarrow{pGH}D_{\Gamma}(o). By Theorem 2.8, this is equivalent to the geometric convergence of \Gamma_{n} to \Gamma.

5.2. Non-equivalence of algebraic and geometric convergence of cocompact Fuchsian groups

Consider an orientable closed surface \Sigma_{g} with a separating closed geodesic \alpha on it such that it divides \Sigma_{g} into the union of \Sigma_{1,0,1} and \Sigma_{g-1,0,1}, see Figure 6 illustrating the case g=3. Pinching \alpha gives rise to an infinite sequence of closed surfaces (\Sigma_{g})_{n} that geometrically converge to a once-punctured torus \Sigma_{1,1}. We can justify it similarly to the previous example. Indeed, denote the corresponding geodesic on (\Sigma_{g})_{n} by \alpha_{n}, where \mathrm{Len}(\alpha_{n})\to 0. For sufficiently large n and an appropriate choice of \varepsilon>0, the thick parts (\Sigma_{g})_{n}^{\geq\varepsilon} have two connected components, where one of them, denoted by T_{n} corresponds to the left piece (\Sigma_{1,0,1})_{n}. Taking the basepoints x_{n}\in Th_{n} so that they stay apart from the geodesic \alpha_{n}, we obtain the pointed Gromov–Hausdorff convergence of ((\Sigma_{g})_{n},x_{n}) to (\Sigma_{1,1},x) for some x\in{\Sigma_{1,1}}. By Theorem 2.8, this is equivalent to the geometric convergence \pi_{1}((\Sigma_{g})_{n})\to\pi_{1}(\Sigma_{1,1})\cong\mathrm{F}_{2}, a free group of rank 2.

On the other hand, all \pi_{1}((\Sigma_{g})_{n}) are isomoprhic to \pi_{1}(\Sigma_{g}), being a one-relator group of four generators, and thus, there are no surjections from \pi_{1}(\Sigma_{1,1})\cong\mathrm{F}_{2}, a free group of rank 2, onto \pi_{1}(\Sigma_{g}).

6. Concluding remarks and further questions

Our main results (Theorems 1.2, 1.3, and 1.5) heavily rely on algebraic convergence, which is essential in the proof of Proposition 3.1. In dimension d=3, this convergence is equivalent to the geometric one, as follows from Theorem 3.2, part (3). On the other hand, in Section 5, we provide an example showing that there are geometrically converging cocompact Fuchsian groups that do not algebraically converge to the same limit. In Theorem 3.2, part (1), we also showed that infinite-covolume lattices in \mathrm{Isom}(\mathbb{H}^{d}), d\geq 3, cannot geommetrically converge to a lattice, while it can happen if d=2 (see an example in Section 5). These phenomena motivate the following questions which we state below.

Let \Gamma_{n} be a sequence of finitely generated Fuchsian groups that geometrically converge to a lattice \Gamma<\mathrm{PSL}_{2}(\mathbb{R}). Assume that the measures \mu_{n} are supported on generating sets S_{n} (of \Gamma_{n}) that converge geometrically to a generating set S of \Gamma such that for each sequence of generators s_{n}\in S_{n} converging to a generator s\in S, the corresponding numbers p_{n} on s_{n} also converge to some number p on s.

Question 6.1.

Is it true that h_{n}\to h? Is it true at least when all but finitely many \Gamma_{n} are lattices?

A positive answer to the first question aims to replace Proposition 3.1 in the proof of the singularity conjecture for groups that are geometrically close to a group for which the singularity conjecture is proven.

Question 6.2.

Assume that all but finitely many \Gamma_{n} are infinite-covolume. Does it imply that the hitting measures \nu_{\mu_{n}} are singular with respect to the Patterson–Sullivan measure \kappa on \partial\mathbb{H}^{2}=\mathbb{S}^{1}?

Assuming a positive answer to Question 6.1, we can answer Question 6.2 in affirmative using the fact that h_{n}\to h, \ell_{n}\to\ell, and v_{n}\to v (the convergence of critical exponents follows from [30] and [31] as explained in [21, Corollary 4.7 (b)]).
