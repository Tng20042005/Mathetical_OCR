On Landis’ conjecture in the plane for
real-valued potentials with decay

Blair Davey Department of Mathematical Sciences, Montana State University, Bozeman, MT, 59717 blairdavey@montana.edu
Abstract.

We investigate the quantitative unique continuation properties of real-valued solutions to planar Schrödinger equations with potential functions that exhibit pointwise decay at infinity. That is, for equations of the form -\Delta u+Vu=0 in \mathbb{R}^{2}, where \left|V(z)\right|\lesssim\left<z\right>^{-N} for some N>0, we prove that real-valued solutions satisfy exponential decay estimates with a rate that depends explicitly on N. Examples show that the estimates established here are essentially sharp. The case of N=0 corresponds to the Landis conjecture, which was proved for real-valued solutions in the plane in [LMNN20], while the case of N<0 was previously investigated by the author in [Dav24]. Here, the proof techniques rely on the ideas presented in [LMNN20] combined with conformal transformations and an iteration scheme.

1. Introduction

This article is concerned with the quantitative unique continuation of solutions to elliptic Schrödinger equations in the plane. We consider equations with potentials that exhibits pointwise decay at infinity. The main result is a nearly sharp estimate for the optimal rate of decay at infinity for real-valued solutions. This theorem may be interpreted as a quantitative Landis-type theorem.

In the late 1960s, E. M. Landis [KL88] conjectured that if u and V are bounded functions that satisfy

(1.1) -\Delta u+Vu=0\;\text{ in }\,\mathbb{R}^{n},

and u decays faster than exponentially, i.e., \left|u(x)\right|\lesssim\exp\left(-c\left|x\right|^{1+}\right), then it follows that u\equiv 0. This conjecture was later disproved by Meshkov [M92] who constructed non-trivial complex-valued functions u and V that solve (1.1) in \mathbb{R}^{2}, where V is bounded and \left|u(x)\right|\lesssim\exp\left(-c\left|x\right|^{4/3}\right). Using Carleman estimate techniques, Meshkov also proved a qualitative unique continuation result: If (1.1) holds, where V is bounded and u satisfies a decay estimate of the form \left|u\left(x\right)\right|\lesssim\exp\left(-c\left|x\right|^{4/3+}\right), then necessarily u\equiv 0.

In their work on Anderson localization [BK05], Bourgain and Kenig established a quantitative version of Meshkov’s result. As a first step in their proof, they used three-ball inequalities derived from Carleman estimates to establish order of vanishing estimates for local solutions to Schrödinger equations of the form (1.1). Then, through a scaling argument, they proved a quantitative unique continuation result. More specifically, they showed that if u and V are bounded and satisfy (1.1), and u is normalized so that \left|u(0)\right|\geq 1, then for sufficiently large values of R,

(1.2) \inf_{|x_{0}|=R}\left\|u\right\|_{L^{\infty}\left(B_{1}(x_{0})\right)}\geq\exp% {(-CR^{\beta}\log R)},

where \beta=\frac{4}{3}. Since \frac{4}{3}>1, the constructions of Meshkov, in combination with the qualitative and quantitative unique continuation theorems just described, indicate that Landis’ conjecture cannot be true for complex-valued solutions in \mathbb{R}^{2}. However, at the time, Landis’ conjecture still remained open in the real-valued and higher-dimensional settings.

The first significant step towards resolving Landis’ conjecture in the real-valued planar setting was made by Kenig, Silvestre and Wang in [KSW15] where they proved a quantitative form of Landis’ conjecture under the assumption that the zeroth-order term satisfies V\geq 0 a.e. The techniques in [KSW15] exploit the relationship between real-valued solutions to second-order elliptic PDEs in the plane and solutions to complex-valued Beltrami equations. Using similar ideas, analogous results were established in the settings with drift terms [KW15], variable coefficients [DKW17], singular lower-order terms [DW20], and when V_{-} exhibits decay at infinity [DKW19, Dav20a]. Then, in [LMNN20], Logunov, Malinnikova, Nadirashvili, and Nazarov proved Landis’ conjecture in the real-valued planar setting. Their proof uses the nodal structure of the domain along with a domain reduction technique to eliminate any sign condition on the zeroth-order term. Many of the ideas from [LMNN20] are used in this article.

In [Dav14], we studied the quantitative unique continuation properties of solutions to elliptic equations of the form

\Delta u+W\cdot\nabla u+Vu=\lambda u\;\text{ in }\;\mathbb{R}^{n},

where V and W exhibit pointwise decay at infinity, and \lambda\in\mathbb{C}. With \left<x\right>=\sqrt{1+\left|x\right|^{2}}, it was shown that if \left|V\left(x\right)\right|\lesssim\left<x\right>^{-N} and \left|W\left(x\right)\right|\lesssim\left<x\right>^{-P} for N,P\geq 0, then the quantitative estimate (1.2) holds with \beta=\max\left\{1,\frac{4-2N}{3},2-2P\right\} and \log R replaced by a different slowly-decaying function. These quantitative estimates were generalized in [LW14], where Lin and Wang proved analogous estimates for solutions to the corresponding equations with variable-coefficient leading terms. Qualitative estimates for similar equations are given in [CS97]. The constructions presented in [Dav14, Dav15] show that the estimates described in this paragraph are sharp.

In [Dav24], we studied quantitative unique continuation at infinity for real-valued solutions to (1.1) when n=2 and the potential exhibits growth at infinity, i.e., \left|V(z)\right|\lesssim\left|z\right|^{N} for N>0. The techniques in that article rely heavily on the ideas in [LMNN20] and careful scaling arguments. Here, we address the more difficult setting where the potential exhibits decay at infinity. The precise statement of the main theorem is as follows.

Theorem 1 (Main Theorem).

For some a_{0}\geq 1 and N\in(0,2), let V:\mathbb{R}^{2}\to\mathbb{R} satisfy

(1.3) \left|V(w)\right|\leq a_{0}^{2}\left<w\right>^{-N}.

Assume that u:\mathbb{R}^{2}\to\mathbb{R} is a solution to

(1.4) -\Delta u+Vu=0\,\text{ in }\mathbb{R}^{2}

with the properties that

(1.5) \left|u(0)\right|=1

and for each w\in\mathbb{R}^{2},

(1.6) \left|u(w)\right|\leq\exp\left(c_{0}\left|w\right|^{1-\frac{N}{2}}\right).

For every \varepsilon\in\left(0,\frac{N}{2}\right), there exists {R}_{0}(N,a_{0},c_{0},\varepsilon)>0 so that whenever R\geq{R}_{0}, it holds that

(1.7) \inf_{\left|w_{0}\right|=R}\left\|u\right\|_{L^{\infty}\left(B_{1}(w_{0})% \right)}\geq\exp\left(-R^{1-\frac{N}{2}+\varepsilon}\right).

The results of [Dav14] prove an estimate of the form (1.2) with \beta=\frac{4-2N}{3}=\frac{4}{3}\left(1-\frac{N}{2}\right)>1-\frac{N}{2}+\varepsilon. In that article, the assumptions are the same as those in Theorem 1, except that u may be complex-valued. Thus, as in the case of bounded V, Theorem 1 illustrates that better estimates hold in the real-valued planar setting.

As illustrated by the following example, Theorem 1 is sharp (up to \varepsilon) for all N\in(0,2). Fix N\in(0,2), then set u(z)=\exp\left(-\left|z\right|^{1-\frac{N}{2}}\right). A computation shows that u satisfies (1.4) where

V(z):=\left(1-\frac{N}{2}\right)^{2}\left(\left|z\right|^{1-\frac{N}{2}}-1% \right)\left|z\right|^{-1-\frac{N}{2}}

satisfies \left|V(z)\right|\lesssim\left|z\right|^{-N}. On the other hand, for any \beta>0, with u(z)=\left|z\right|^{-\beta} on \left|z\right|>1, we see that \displaystyle\Delta u=\beta^{2}\left|z\right|^{-\beta-2} and therefore u satisfies (1.4) on an exterior domain with V(z):=\beta^{2}\left|z\right|^{-2}. In particular, we may not have exponential behavior when V decays fast enough, which explains why we restrict ourselves to N<2.

To prove Theorem 1, we use an iterative argument that is reminiscent of the one in [Dav14], see also [LW14, DKW19, Dav20a, Dav25]. To initialize the iteration, we apply a quantitative estimate of the form (1.2) with \beta=1. This result, which verifies Landis’ conjecture in the real-valued planar setting, was originally proved by Logunov, Malinnikova, Nadirashvili, and Nazarov in [LMNN20], and we formulate it in Theorem 3 below. The iteration argument then relies on repeated applications of Proposition 2 which is proved using the ideas from [LMNN20]. Roughly speaking, Proposition 2 shows that if an estimate like (1.2) holds with \beta=\beta_{0}, then for some x_{1} with \left|x_{1}\right|\gg\left|x_{0}\right|, another estimate like (1.2) holds with x_{0} replaced by x_{1} and \beta=\beta_{1}\in\left[1,\beta_{0}\right]. When \beta_{0}=1, Proposition 2 isn’t useful, but when \beta_{0}>1, we can decrease the exponent, i.e., make \beta_{1}<\beta_{0}. Therefore, to benefit from the iteration argument, we need to transform to a situation where \beta_{0}>1. We observe that if u is composed with the real-variable version of the conformal transformation z\mapsto z^{\alpha}, then the new function also satisfies a Schrödinger equation. By choosing \alpha>1 appropriately, we can ensure that the new potential function is bounded and that the new solution function satisfies a version of (1.2) with \beta>1. By repeatedly applying Proposition 2 to the transformed equation, we can make \beta arbitrarily close to 1. Finally, to reach the conclusion, we undo the change of variables.

We use the notation B_{r}(z) to denote a ball of radius r>0 centered at the point z, abbreviated by B_{r} when the center is clear. Generic constants are denoted by c,C and may change from line to line without comment. Specific constants will be indicated by subscripts.

The article is organized as follows. In Section 2, we present a unique continuation theorem for harmonic functions in punctured domains. The content of this section is very similar to [LMNN20, Section 5] and [Dav24, Section 2]. The iterative result described by Proposition 2 is the content of Section 3. Proposition 2 is a three-ball inequality for solutions to Schrödinger equations and its proof relies on the results from Section 2. In Section 4, we introduce the real-valued versions of z\mapsto z^{\alpha} and record some of their properties. In particular, we show how solutions behave when they are composed with these transformations. Finally, the proof of Theorem 1 is presented in Section 5.

2. Decay properties of harmonic functions in punctured domains

In this section, we present and prove quantitative unique continuation results (in the form of three-ball inequalities) for harmonic functions in punctured domains. We recall the following application of the Harnack inequality which appears in [LMNN20, Claim 5.2] and is repeated in [Dav24, Lemma 2.1].

Lemma 1 (Harnack application).

Let \left\{D_{j}\right\} be a finite collection of 100-separated unit disks in the plane. Assume that h is real-valued and harmonic in \mathbb{R}^{2}\setminus\bigcup D_{j} and that for each index j, h doesn’t change sign in 5D_{j}\setminus D_{j}. There exists an absolute constant C_{H}\geq 10 for which

  1. (1)

    \displaystyle\max_{\partial\left(3D_{j}\right)}\left|h\right|\leq C_{H}\min_{% \partial\left(3D_{j}\right)}\left|h\right|

  2. (2)

    \displaystyle\max_{\partial\left(3D_{j}\right)}\left|\nabla h\right|\leq C_{H}% \min_{\partial\left(3D_{j}\right)}\left|h\right|.

Proof.

An application of the Harnack inequality shows that there exists C_{H}>0 so that for every j

\displaystyle\max_{\partial\left(3D_{j}\right)}\left|h\right| \displaystyle\leq\sup_{4D_{j}\setminus 2D_{j}}\left|h\right|\leq C_{H}\inf_{4D% _{j}\setminus 2D_{j}}\left|h\right|\leq C_{H}\min_{\partial\left(3D_{j}\right)% }\left|h\right|.

For each z\in\partial\left(3D_{j}\right), since h doesn’t change signs in B_{2}(z), an application of Cauchy’s inequality as in [HL11, Lemma 1.11] shows that \left|\nabla h(z)\right|\leq\left|h(z)\right| and the conclusion follows. ∎

Now we state and prove the main result of this section. The following is a slight modification of [Dav24, Lemma 2.1], which resembles the result [LMNN20, Theorem 5.3].

Proposition 1 (Three-ball inequality for harmonic function in punctured domain).

Let \left\{D_{j}\right\} be a finite collection of 100-separated unit disks in the plane for which 0\notin\bigcup 3D_{j}. For some R\geq 2^{10}, let h be a harmonic function in B_{R}\setminus\bigcup D_{j} with the property that for each index j, h doesn’t change sign in \left(5D_{j}\setminus D_{j}\right)\cap B_{R}. Assume that for some S\in\left(2^{8},\frac{R}{2}\right) and some M>\log\left(16R\right), with T:=R-\frac{S}{32}, it holds that

(2.1) \sup_{B_{T}\setminus\bigcup 3D_{j}}\left|h\right|\geq e^{-M}\sup_{B_{R}% \setminus\bigcup 3D_{j}}\left|h\right|.

Then for every r\in\left(0,\frac{R}{2^{10}}\right), we have

(2.2) \sup_{B_{r}\setminus\bigcup 3D_{j}}\left|h\right|\geq\left(\frac{16r}{R}\right% )^{\kappa}\sup_{B_{T}\setminus\bigcup 3D_{j}}\left|h\right|,

where \kappa(R,S,M)=\max\left\{6C_{H}R,2^{13}MRS^{-1}\right\} and C_{H}\geq 10 is from Lemma 1.

Remark 2.

In contrast to [Dav24, Lemma 2.1], we no longer assume that S is bounded from below with respect to R and we impose a lower bound on M. This flexibility is useful for the iterative argument.

Proof.

We may assume without loss of generality that

(2.3) \sup_{B_{T}\setminus\bigcup 3D_{j}}\left|h\right|=1.

Set k=\max\left\{2C_{H}R,2^{10}MRS^{-1}\right\}. For the sake of contradiction, assume that

(2.4) \sup_{B_{r}\setminus\bigcup 3D_{j}}\left|h\right|\leq\left(\frac{16r}{R}\right% )^{3k}.

Define the punctured annular region

\Omega:=\left\{\frac{r}{2}<\left|z\right|<R-1\right\}\setminus\bigcup 3D_{j}

Observe that f is analytic in \Omega and \left|f(z)\right|=\left|\nabla h(z)\right|\left|z\right|^{-k}. We’ll analyze the behavior of f over \Omega. We begin with bounding h and \nabla h over the innermost and outermost parts of the boundary of \Omega.

Let W_{1} be the connected component of \partial\Omega that intersects the inner circle \left\{\left|z\right|=\frac{r}{2}\right\}. If z\in W_{1}, then there are three cases to consider:

  • (a)

    \left|z\right|\neq\frac{r}{2}.

  • (b)

    \left|z\right|=\frac{r}{2} and there exists j for which z\in 4D_{j}\setminus 3D_{j}.

  • (c)

    \left|z\right|=\frac{r}{2} and z\cap 4D_{j} is empty for all j.

Case (a): There exists j for which z\in\partial\left(3D_{j}\right) and 3D_{j}\cap\left\{\left|z\right|=\frac{r}{2}\right\} is non-empty. An application of Lemma 1 combined with the fact that \partial\left(3D_{j}\right)\cap B_{R} is non-empty shows that

\displaystyle\left|h(z)\right|,\left|\nabla h(z)\right| \displaystyle\leq C_{H}\min_{\partial\left(3D_{j}\right)}\left|h\right|\leq C_% {H}\sup_{B_{r}\setminus\bigcup 3D_{j}}\left|h\right|\leq C_{H}\left(\frac{16r}% {R}\right)^{3k},

where the last inequality follows from (2.4).
Case (b): Since h doesn’t change signs in B_{1}(z), then an application of [HL11, Lemma 1.11] shows that

\displaystyle\left|\nabla h(z)\right| \displaystyle\leq 2\left|h(z)\right|\leq 2\sup_{B_{R}\setminus\bigcup 3D_{j}}% \left|h\right|\leq 2\left(\frac{16r}{R}\right)^{3k},

where the second inequality uses that z\notin\bigcup 3D_{j} and we have again applied (2.4).

\displaystyle\left|\nabla h(z)\right| \displaystyle\leq\frac{2}{d}\sup_{B_{d}\left(z\right)}\left|h\right|\leq\frac{% 2}{d}\sup_{B_{R}\setminus\bigcup 3D_{j}}\left|h\right|\leq\frac{2}{d}\left(% \frac{16r}{R}\right)^{3k}.

If d=1, since k>1, then \displaystyle\frac{2}{d}\left(\frac{16r}{R}\right)^{k}=2\left(\frac{16r}{R}% \right)^{k}<\frac{32r}{R}<\frac{1}{2^{5}}<\frac{1}{2}. (2.5) \displaystyle\sup_{W_{1}}\left|h\right|,\;\sup_{W_{1}}\left|\nabla h\right|% \leq\left(\frac{16r}{R}\right)^{2k}.

  • (a)

    there exists j for which z\in 4D_{j}.

  • (b)

    \left|z\right|=R-1 and z\cap 4D_{j} is empty for all j.

Case (a): Since h doesn’t change sign in B_{1}(z), then an application of [HL11, Lemma 1.10] shows that

\displaystyle\left|\nabla h(z)\right| \displaystyle\leq 2\left|h(z)\right|\leq 2\sup_{B_{R}\setminus\bigcup 3D_{j}}% \left|h\right|\leq 2e^{M},

where we have applied (2.1).
Case (b): Since B_{1}(z)\subset B_{R}\setminus\bigcup 3D_{j}, then

\displaystyle\left|\nabla h(z)\right| \displaystyle\leq 2\sup_{B_{1}\left(z\right)}\left|h\right|\leq 2\sup_{B_{R}% \setminus\bigcup 3D_{j}}\left|h\right|\leq 2e^{M}.

By combining both cases, we see that

(2.6) \sup_{W_{2}}\left|h\right|\leq e^{M},\;\sup_{W_{2}}\left|\nabla h\right|\leq 2% e^{M}.

We now use these estimates on h from above to understand the behavior of the analytic function \displaystyle f=\frac{h_{x}-ih_{y}}{z^{k}}. Define the set \Omega_{1}\subset\Omega as

\Omega_{1}:=\left\{\frac{r}{2}<\left|z\right|<T\right\}\setminus\bigcup 3D_{j}.

Using containment, assumption (2.4), and our normalization (2.3), we see that

\displaystyle\sup_{B_{\frac{r}{2}}\setminus\bigcup 3D_{j}}\left|h\right|\leq% \sup_{B_{r}\setminus\bigcup 3D_{j}}\left|h\right|\leq\left(\frac{16r}{R}\right% )^{3k}<1=\sup_{B_{T}\setminus\bigcup 3D_{j}}\left|h\right|.

\displaystyle\sup_{W_{1}}\left|h\right|\leq\left(\frac{16r}{R}\right)^{2k}<% \frac{1}{2},

\displaystyle\log\left(\frac{R-7}{R-\frac{S}{32}}\right) \displaystyle=\log\left(1+\frac{\frac{S}{32}-7}{R-\frac{S}{32}}\right)\geq\log% \left(1+\frac{S}{2^{8}R}\right)\geq\frac{S}{2^{8}R}\left(1-\frac{S}{2^{9}R}% \right).

where the second inequality is by assumption. Therefore, (2.10) holds. Combining (2.10) with (2.9) and (2.7) shows that

\displaystyle\max_{W_{2}}\left|f\right|<\sup_{\Omega}\left|f\right|.

Since f is a holomorphic function in \Omega, then the maximum principle guarantees that \displaystyle\sup_{\Omega}\left|f\right|=\sup_{\partial\Omega}\left|f\right|. As shown above, the maximum doesn’t occur on W_{1} or W_{2}, so there must exist a disk 3D_{j}\subset\left\{\frac{r}{2}<\left|z\right|<R-1\right\} for which \displaystyle\sup_{\Omega}\left|f\right|=\sup_{\partial\left(3D_{j}\right)}% \left|f\right|.

Considering only the disks D_{j} for which 3D_{j}\subset\left\{\frac{r}{2}<\left|z\right|<R-1\right\}, define z_{j}\in\partial\left(3D_{j}\right) to be the point that is closest to the origin, i.e. has the smallest modulus. (2.11) m_{j_{0}}\left|z_{j_{0}}\right|^{-k}=\max_{j}m_{j}\left|z_{j}\right|^{-k}

and let j_{1} be the index for which

\sup_{\Omega}\left|f\right|=\sup_{\partial\left(3D_{j_{1}}\right)}\left|f% \right|.

(2.12) \left|\nabla h(z)\right|\left|z\right|^{-k}\leq\sup_{\Omega}\left|f\right|=% \sup_{\partial\left(3D_{j_{1}}\right)}\left|\nabla h(z)\right|\left|z_{j_{1}}% \right|^{-k}\leq C_{H}m_{j_{1}}\left|z_{j_{1}}\right|^{-k}\leq C_{H}m_{j_{0}}% \left|z_{j_{0}}\right|^{-k},

3. The iterative proposition

Here we present the proposition that is used repeatedly in the iteration argument. This unique continuation result takes the form of a three-ball inequality for solutions to Schrödinger equations. The techniques used to prove this theorem are very similar to those that appear in the proofs of [LMNN20, Theorem 2.2] and [Dav24, Theorem 1.2].

Proposition 2 (Iterative Proposition).

with

for some c_{1}\geq 1. Assume that for some S\in\left(2^{8},\frac{R}{2}\right), there exists z_{0}\in\overline{B}_{R-S} and L\geq 0 such that

Then there exists r_{0}(a,R)>1 so that whenever r\in(0,r_{0}), it holds that

Define \Omega=B_{R}\setminus\left(F_{0}\bigcup F_{1}\right) and \Omega_{1}=B_{R}\setminus F_{1}. As shown in [LMNN20, §3.1], there exists a universal constant c_{P} (that depends on c_{s}) so that \Omega has Poincaré constant bounded above by c_{P}\rho^{2}. (3.4) \rho=\varepsilon a^{-1}.

An application of the arguments in [LMNN20, §3.2] then shows that there exists \phi:\Omega\to\mathbb{R} with the properties that

where c_{b} is a universal constant that depends on c_{P}, and we have used (3.4). 4. Transformation maps

In this section, we introduce the transformation maps that serve as real-valued versions of the conformal maps z\mapsto z^{\alpha}. Once the maps are defined, we show how they transform solutions to elliptic PDEs and how they transform balls. These results will be used at the beginning and at the end of the proof of Theorem 1.

Given z=(x,y)\in\mathbb{R}^{2} in Cartesian coordinates, the polar coordinates for z are (r,\theta)\in\mathbb{R}_{+}\times(-\pi,\pi], where r=\sqrt{x^{2}+y^{2}} and \theta=\operatorname{sgn}(y)\arccos\left(\frac{x}{r}\right). Let \mathbb{R}^{2}_{+}=\left\{z=(r,\theta):r>0,\theta\in(-\frac{\pi}{2},\frac{\pi}% {2})\right\} denote the right half-plane. For \alpha\in(1,\infty), we define T_{\alpha}:\mathbb{R}^{2}\to\mathbb{R}^{2} to be the map associated to the conformal transformation on \mathbb{C} given by z\mapsto z^{\alpha}. In polar coordinates, T_{\alpha} is described as

(4.1) T_{\alpha}(r,\theta)=(r^{\alpha},\alpha\theta).

To avoid continuity issues at \theta=\pi, we restrict the domain and only consider T_{\alpha}:\mathbb{R}^{2}_{+}\to\mathbb{R}^{2}.

We recall the following result originally proved in [LMNN20], see also [Dav24, Theorem 1.1] in the case where N=0 for this formulation. This estimate serves as the initialization step in our iteration argument.

Theorem 3 (Initialization step).

For some a_{0}>0, let V:\mathbb{R}^{2}\to\mathbb{R} satisfy \left\|V\right\|_{L^{\infty}}\leq a_{0}^{2}. Assume that u:\mathbb{R}^{2}\to\mathbb{R} is a solution to (1.4) that satisfies (1.5) and for each w\in\mathbb{R}^{2},

\left|u(w)\right|\leq\exp\left(c_{0}\left|w\right|\right).

Then there exists constants \overline{C}_{0}=\overline{C}_{0}\left(a_{0},c_{0}\right)>0 and \overline{R}_{0}>0 so that whenever R\geq\overline{R}_{0}, it holds that

(5.1) \inf_{\left|w_{0}\right|=R}\left\|u\right\|_{L^{\infty}\left(B_{1}(w_{0})% \right)}\geq\exp\left(-\overline{C}_{0}R\log^{\frac{3}{2}}R\right).

Observe that if u satisfies the hypothesis of Theorem 1, then it also satisfies the hypotheses of Theorem 3. We now have all of the tools we need to prove Theorem 1.

Proof of Theorem 1.

With N\in(0,2) as given, let \alpha=\left(1-\frac{N}{2}\right)^{-1}=\frac{2}{2-N}\in(1,\infty). Set \delta=\min\left\{\frac{\varepsilon}{2-N},1\right\}. With r_{\alpha} from Lemma 3, \overline{C}_{0}(a_{0},c_{0}) and \overline{R}_{0} from Theorem 3, and \hat{R}_{0}(a) from Proposition 2, define \bar{r}_{1}\geq\max\left\{r_{\alpha},\overline{R}_{0}^{\frac{1}{\alpha}},\hat{% R}_{0}\left(a_{0}\alpha\right)\right\} as small as possible so that each of the following conditions hold:

(5.2) \displaystyle r^{\delta}\geq\overline{C}_{0}\alpha^{\frac{3}{2}}\log^{\frac{3}% {2}}r
(5.3) \displaystyle r\log r\geq c_{d}
(5.4) \displaystyle r^{\frac{\delta}{1+\delta}}\geq\frac{3a_{0}\alpha C_{H}}{2^{14}% \left(5c_{0}+4\right)}\sqrt{\frac{C_{K}}{32\ln 2}}\sqrt{\log r}
(5.5) \displaystyle r^{\frac{\delta^{2}}{2}}\geq\left[2^{14}\left(5c_{0}+4\right)+% \frac{2}{5}\right]\log r
(5.6) \displaystyle r^{\frac{5\delta^{2}}{6}}\geq\left[2^{14}\left(5c_{0}+4\right)+% \frac{2}{5}\right]\log\left[\frac{4\alpha}{\sqrt{3}}\left(\frac{3}{2}r\right)^% {\alpha}\right].

Here C_{K} and c_{d} are universal constants from Proposition 2 and C_{H} is the universal Harnack constant from Lemma 1.

For each k\in\mathbb{N}, define

\alpha_{k}=\frac{2+(k-1)N}{2+(k-2)N}>1.

Observe that \alpha_{1}=\alpha, \alpha_{k+1}=2-\frac{1}{\alpha_{k}}<\alpha_{k}, and \displaystyle\lim_{k\to\infty}\alpha_{k}=1. Choose \ell\in\mathbb{N} so that \frac{N}{2+\left(\ell-2\right)N}\leq\delta<\frac{N}{2+\left(\ell-3\right)N} or \alpha_{\ell}\leq 1+\delta<\alpha_{\ell-1}. For each k\in\left\{1,2,\ldots,\ell-1\right\}, recursively define \bar{r}_{k+1}=\bar{r}_{k}^{\alpha_{k}+\delta}+\frac{1}{2}\bar{r}_{k}-1, where \bar{r}_{1} is from above. Take w_{0}\in\mathbb{R}^{2} with \left|w_{0}\right|\geq R_{0}:=\bar{r}_{\ell}^{\alpha}. After a rotation, we may assume that w_{0} belongs to the positive x-axis. Choose r_{1}\geq\bar{r}_{1} so that with r_{k+1}=r_{k}^{\alpha_{k}+\delta}+\frac{1}{2}r_{k}-1 for each k\in\left\{1,\ldots,\ell-1\right\}, it holds that \left|w_{0}\right|=r_{\ell}^{\alpha}. Set z_{k}=\left(r_{k},0\right)\in\mathbb{R}^{2}_{+} for each k\in\left\{1,\ldots,\ell-1\right\} so that

(5.7) \left|z_{k}\right|=\left|z_{k-1}\right|^{\alpha_{k-1}+\delta}+\frac{1}{2}\left% |z_{k-1}\right|-1.

With \alpha=\alpha_{1}>1, let T_{\alpha}:\mathbb{R}^{2}_{+}\to\mathbb{R}^{2} be as given in (4.1), see Section 4. Define v:\mathbb{R}^{2}_{+}\to\mathbb{R} by v(z)=u(T_{\alpha}(z)).

Claim.

For every k\in\left\{1,\ldots,\ell-1\right\},

(5.8) \displaystyle\left\|v\right\|_{L^{\infty}\left(B_{1}(z_{k})\right)} \displaystyle\geq\exp\left(-\left|z_{k}\right|^{\alpha_{k}+\delta}\right).

To prove the claim, we proceed by induction. Define w_{1}=T_{\alpha}(z_{1})=(r_{1}^{\alpha},0). Since r_{1}\geq r_{\alpha}, Lemma 3 implies that B_{1}(w_{1})\subset T_{\alpha}(B_{1}(z_{1})) and then

\displaystyle\left\|v\right\|_{L^{\infty}\left(B_{1}(z_{1})\right)} \displaystyle=\left\|u\circ T_{\alpha}\right\|_{L^{\infty}\left(B_{1}(z_{1})% \right)}=\left\|u\right\|_{L^{\infty}\left(T_{\alpha}(B_{1}(z_{1}))\right)}% \geq\left\|u\right\|_{L^{\infty}\left(B_{1}(w_{1})\right)}.

As \left|w_{1}\right|=r_{1}^{\alpha}\geq\overline{R}_{0}, an application of (5.1) from Theorem 3 shows that

\left\|u\right\|_{L^{\infty}(B_{1}(w_{1}))}\geq\exp\left(-\overline{C}_{0}% \left|w_{1}\right|\log^{\frac{3}{2}}\left|w_{1}\right|\right).

Because \left|w_{1}\right|=\left|z_{1}\right|^{\alpha}, then \overline{C}_{0}\left|w_{1}\right|\log^{\frac{3}{2}}\left|w_{1}\right|=% \overline{C}_{0}\left|z_{1}\right|^{\alpha}\log^{\frac{3}{2}}\left|z_{1}\right% |^{\alpha}=\overline{C}_{0}\alpha^{\frac{3}{2}}\left|z_{1}\right|^{\alpha}\log% ^{\frac{3}{2}}\left|z_{1}\right|\leq\left|z_{1}\right|^{\alpha+\delta}, where we have used (5.2) to reach the last bound. Combining these observations shows that

\displaystyle\left\|v\right\|_{L^{\infty}\left(B_{1}(z_{1})\right)} \displaystyle\geq\left\|u\right\|_{L^{\infty}\left(B_{1}(w_{1})\right)}\geq% \exp\left(-\overline{C}_{0}\left|w_{1}\right|\log^{\frac{3}{2}}\left|w_{1}% \right|\right)\geq\exp\left(-\left|z_{1}\right|^{\alpha+\delta}\right).

In other words, (5.8) holds for k=1.

Now assume that (5.8) holds for k-1\in\mathbb{N}. Define S_{k}=\frac{1}{2}\left|z_{k-1}\right| and R_{k}=\left|z_{k-1}\right|^{\alpha_{k-1}+\delta}. Comparing with (5.7), we see that \left|z_{k}\right|=R_{k}+S_{k}-1. Since B_{1}(z_{k-1})\subset B_{R_{k}-S_{k}}(z_{k}), then the inductive hypothesis shows that there exists z_{0}\in\overline{B}_{R_{k}-S_{k}}(z_{k}) so that

\displaystyle\left|v(z_{0})\right|\geq\exp\left(-\left|z_{k-1}\right|^{\alpha_% {k-1}+\delta}\right)=\exp\left(-R_{k}\right).

The bound in (1.6) shows that \displaystyle\left|v(z)\right|=\left|u(T_{\alpha}(z))\right|\leq\exp\left(c_{0% }\left|z\right|^{\alpha\left(1-\frac{N}{2}\right)}\right)=\exp\left(c_{0}\left% |z\right|\right) from which it follows that

\displaystyle\left\|v\right\|_{L^{\infty}\left(B_{R_{k}}(z_{k})\right)}\leq% \exp\left(c_{0}\left(\left|z_{k}\right|+R_{k}\right)\right)\leq\exp\left(\frac% {5c_{0}}{2}R_{k}\right).

Let W:\mathbb{R}^{2}_{+}\to\mathbb{R} be given by W(z)=\alpha^{2}\left|z\right|^{2\alpha-2}V(T_{\alpha}(z))=\alpha^{2}\left|w% \right|^{2-\frac{2}{\alpha}}V(w) so that by (1.3),

\displaystyle\left|W(z)\right|=\alpha^{2}\left|w\right|^{2-\frac{2}{\alpha}}% \left|V(w)\right|\leq a_{0}^{2}\alpha^{2}\left|w\right|^{2-N-\frac{2}{\alpha}}% =\left(a_{0}\alpha\right)^{2}.

An application of Lemma 2 with assumption (1.4) shows that

-\Delta v+Wv=0\;\text{ in }\mathbb{R}^{2}_{+}.

As R_{k}\geq\bar{r}_{1} implies that R_{k}\geq\hat{R}_{0}(a_{0}\alpha) and S_{k}\in\left(2^{8},\frac{R_{k}}{2}\right), Proposition 2 is applicable with a=a_{0}\alpha, R=R_{k}, S=S_{k}, L=R_{k}, and c_{1}=\frac{5c_{0}}{2}. With r=1, Proposition 2 shows that

(5.9) \displaystyle\left\|v\right\|_{L^{\infty}(B_{1}(z_{k}))} \displaystyle\geq\exp\left(-\tau_{k}(R_{k})\log R_{k}\right),

where

(5.10) \displaystyle\tau_{k}(R_{k}) \displaystyle=\max\left\{3a_{0}\alpha C_{H}\sqrt{\frac{C_{K}}{32\ln 2}}R_{k}% \sqrt{\log R_{k}},2^{15}\left(\frac{5c_{0}}{2}+1+\frac{c_{d}}{R_{k}\log R_{k}}% \right)R_{k}^{2-\frac{1}{\alpha_{k-1}+\delta}}\right\}
\displaystyle+\frac{1}{5}\left(R_{k}+\frac{c_{d}}{\log R_{k}}\right).

Because R_{k}\geq\bar{r}_{1} and \alpha_{k-1}>1, conditions (5.3) and (5.4) imply that

(5.11) \displaystyle\tau_{k}(R_{k}) \displaystyle\leq\left[2^{14}\left(5c_{0}+4\right)+\frac{2}{5}\right]R_{k}^{2-% \frac{1}{\alpha_{k-1}+\delta}}

while condition (5.5) implies that

\left[2^{14}\left(5c_{0}+4\right)+\frac{2}{5}\right]\log R_{k}\leq R_{k}^{% \frac{\delta^{2}}{2}}.

Since \alpha>1 and \delta\leq 1 implies that 2-\frac{1}{\alpha+\delta}+\frac{\delta^{2}}{2}<2-\frac{1}{\alpha}+\delta, then combining these bounds shows that

\tau_{k}(R_{k})\log R_{k}\leq R_{k}^{2-\frac{1}{\alpha_{k-1}}+\delta}=R_{k}^{% \alpha_{k}+\delta}.

Returning to (5.9), we conclude that

\displaystyle\left\|v\right\|_{L^{\infty}(B_{1}(z_{k}))} \displaystyle\geq\exp\left(-R_{k}^{\alpha_{k}+\delta}\right)\geq\exp\left(-% \left|z_{k}\right|^{\alpha_{k}+\delta}\right),

establishing the claim given by (5.8).

Next we consider the behavior of v on a ball centered at z_{\ell}. The arguments in the previous paragraph show that Proposition 2 is applicable with a=a_{0}\alpha, R=R_{\ell}, S=S_{\ell}, L=R_{\ell}, and c_{1}=\frac{5c_{0}}{2}. In particular, with \displaystyle r=\tilde{r}:=\frac{\left|z_{\ell}\right|^{1-\alpha}}{2\sqrt{3}\alpha}, Proposition 2 shows that

\displaystyle\left\|u\right\|_{L^{\infty}(B_{1}(T_{\alpha}(z_{\ell})))}\geq% \left\|u\right\|_{L^{\infty}(T_{\alpha}\left(B_{\tilde{r}}(z_{\ell})\right))}=% \left\|v\right\|_{L^{\infty}(B_{\tilde{r}}(z_{\ell}))} \displaystyle\geq\exp\left(-\left|z_{\ell}\right|^{1+2\delta}\right)=\exp\left% (-r_{\ell}^{1+2\delta}\right).

Because we assumed that w_{0} belongs to the positive x-axis and has \left|w_{0}\right|=r_{\ell}^{\alpha}, then T_{\alpha}(z_{\ell})=\left(r_{\ell}^{\alpha},0\right)=w_{0}.
