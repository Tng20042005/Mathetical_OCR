Abstract

In this paper, for every n\in\mathbb{N}, the following relationships between the functions K_{b}(n) and K_{e}(n) and the Bernoulli and Euler numbers are proved:

\displaystyle B_{2n} \displaystyle=-\frac{(2n)!}{2^{2n}-2}\times K_{b}(n)
\displaystyle E_{2n} \displaystyle=(2n)!\times K_{e}(n)

where the functions K_{b} and K_{e} are defined recursively as follows:

\displaystyle K_{b}(0) \displaystyle=K_{e}(0)=1
\displaystyle K_{b}(n) \displaystyle=-\sum_{n^{\prime}=0}^{n-1}\frac{K_{b}(n^{\prime})}{(2(n-n^{% \prime})+1)!},\quad n\geq 1
\displaystyle K_{e}(n) \displaystyle=-\sum_{n^{\prime}=0}^{n-1}\frac{K_{e}(n^{\prime})}{(2(n-n^{% \prime}))!},\quad n\geq 1

Furthermore, we present combinatorial interpretations of these functions in terms of ordered partitions of n, as follows:

\displaystyle K_{b}(n) \displaystyle=\sum_{\lambda\vDash n}\frac{(-1)^{l(\lambda)}}{\prod_{i=1}^{l(% \lambda)}(2b_{i}+1)!},\quad n\geq 1
\displaystyle K_{e}(n) \displaystyle=\sum_{\lambda\vDash n}\frac{(-1)^{l(\lambda)}}{\prod_{i=1}^{l(% \lambda)}(2b_{i})!},\quad n\geq 1

where \lambda=(b_{1},b_{2},\ldots,b_{k})\vDash n and l(\lambda)=k.

1 Introduction

The primary objective of this paper is to develop a unified framework for two fundamental families of numbers, the Bernoulli numbers and the Euler numbers, and to introduce new definitions and computational techniques that arise from this shared structure. By relating these numbers to ordered partitions (compositions) of integers, we reveal a novel connection linking number theory, combinatorics, and special functions. In the sections that follow, we provide the necessary definitions and establish the key relations.

2 Definitions

2.1 Ordered Partitions of n

An ordered partition (composition) of n is a sequence of positive integers \lambda=(b_{1},b_{2},\ldots,b_{k}) such that:

b_{1}+b_{2}+\ldots+b_{k}=n

where the order of the components matters and the number of components (k) is called the length of the partition.

2.2 Bernoulli Numbers

Jacob Bernoulli (1654-1705), a Swiss mathematician and physicist, first systematically studied Bernoulli numbers in 1713. These numbers emerged during his investigations of the sums of powers of natural numbers and became a fundamental tool in number theory and analysis [1]. Over time, the connection of this sequence of numbers with the expansions of many functions, including the zeta function, has been established; thus, Bernoulli numbers hold great importance in mathematics. In general, the sum \sum_{k=1}^{n-1}k^{r} can be written as follows [2]:

\sum_{k=1}^{n-1}k^{r}=\sum_{k=0}^{r}\frac{B_{k}\,r!\,n^{r-k+1}}{k!(r-k+1)!}

where B_{k} is independent of r and are called Bernoulli numbers.

2.3 Euler Numbers

Leonhard Euler (1707-1783) studied Euler numbers in 1755 in his book Institutiones Calculi Differentialis [3]. These numbers, which initially appeared in the context of expansions of trigonometric series, gradually found extensive applications in combinatorics and graph theory. By discovering the connection between these numbers and specific permutations, Euler laid foundations that are still used in combinatorial research today. These numbers form a sequence E_{n} of natural numbers defined by the Taylor series as follows [4]:

\frac{1}{\cosh(t)}=\frac{2}{e^{t}+e^{-t}}=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac{E_{n}}{n!}t% ^{n}

2.4 Zeta Function

The Riemann zeta function \zeta(s) was first studied by Leonhard Euler in the 18th century for real numbers (s>1), but Bernhard Riemann (1826-1866) extended it to complex numbers in 1859 [7].This function is defined for \text{Re(s)}>1 as:

\zeta(s)=\sum_{k=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{k^{s}}

Riemann showed that this function can be analytically continued for all complex numbers except s=1 (where it has a simple pole). This function has a deep connection with the distribution of prime numbers [7].

2.5 Hurwitz Zeta Function

The Hurwitz zeta function, a generalization of the Riemann zeta function, was introduced by Adolf Hurwitz (1859-1919) in 1882. For \text{Re(s)}>1, it is defined as an absolutely convergent series [8]:

\zeta(s,x)=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac{1}{(n+x)^{s}};\quad\frac{d}{dx}\zeta(s,x)=% -s\,\zeta(s+1,x)

This function can be extended to other values of s by analytic continuation, except at s=1 where the function has a simple pole.

Generalized harmonic functions H_{x}^{s} can be expressed via the Hurwitz zeta function:

H_{x}^{s}=\sum_{n=1}^{x}\frac{1}{n^{s}}=\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{n^{s}}-% \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac{1}{(n+x+1)^{s}};
H_{x}^{s}=\zeta(s)-\zeta(s,x+1);
\frac{d}{dx}(H_{x}^{s})=s\,\zeta(s+1,x+1)

2.6 Gamma Function

The Gamma Function was first introduced by the Swiss mathematician Leonhard Euler in his endeavor to generalize the factorial function to non-integer values. Useful details on the origin and evolution of the Gamma function can be found in [5]. Euler introduced the following analytic function during the years 1729 and 1730:

\Gamma(n)=\int_{0}^{1}(-\ln(t))^{n-1}dt,\quad(n>0)

which, by an elementary change of variable, leads to the following equation [5]:

\Gamma(n)=\int_{0}^{\infty}t^{n-1}e^{-t}dt,\quad(n>0)

Connection with the factorial function [5]:

\Gamma(n+1)=n\Gamma(n)=n!

Some specific values [5]:

\Gamma(1)=1,\quad\Gamma\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)=\sqrt{\pi},\quad\Gamma\left(% \frac{3}{2}\right)=\frac{\sqrt{\pi}}{2}

Efforts have been made and formulas have been provided that describe the Gamma function. One such formula for \text{Re}(x)>-\frac{1}{2} in article [6], page 12, is as follows:

\Gamma(x+1)=\frac{\left(x+\frac{1}{2}\right)^{x+\frac{1}{2}}\sqrt{2\pi}}{e^{x+% \frac{1}{2}}}\times e^{-\sum_{j=1}^{\infty}\frac{\zeta(2j,x+1)}{(2j+4j^{2})2^{% 2j}}} (1)

In addition to the Gamma function itself, its logarithmic derivative, the Digamma function, is also very important. To compute this derivative, we proceed as follows.

H_{x}^{2j}=\zeta(2j)-\zeta(2j,x+1);
\Gamma(x+1)=\frac{\left(x+\frac{1}{2}\right)^{x+\frac{1}{2}}\sqrt{2\pi}}{e^{x+% \frac{1}{2}}}\times e^{\sum_{j=1}^{\infty}\frac{H_{x}^{2j}-\zeta(2j)}{(2j+4j^{% 2})2^{2j}}};

From article [9], page 50, we know:

\sum_{j=1}^{\infty}\frac{\zeta(2j)}{(2j+4j^{2})2^{2j}}=\ln\sqrt{\frac{\pi}{e}} (2)

Therefore:

\Gamma(x+1)=\frac{\left(x+\frac{1}{2}\right)^{x+\frac{1}{2}}\sqrt{2}}{e^{x}}% \times e^{\sum_{j=1}^{\infty}\frac{H_{x}^{2j}}{(2j+4j^{2})2^{2j}}};

We define the function A to simplify the differentiation of the Gamma function as follows:

A=\sum_{j=1}^{\infty}\frac{H_{x}^{2j}}{(2j+4j^{2})2^{2j}} (3)

Therefore:

\Gamma(x+1)=\frac{\left(x+\frac{1}{2}\right)^{x+\frac{1}{2}}\sqrt{2}}{e^{x-A}}; (4)
\frac{d}{dx}\Gamma(x)=\frac{\left(x+\frac{1}{2}\right)^{x+\frac{1}{2}}\sqrt{2}% }{e^{x-A}}\left(\ln\left(x+\frac{1}{2}\right)+\frac{dA}{dx}\right)

2.7 Digamma Function

The Digamma Function is obtained by differentiating the logarithm of the Gamma function as follows:

\frac{d}{dx}\Gamma(x)=\Gamma(x)\psi(x);
\Gamma(x+1)\psi(x+1)=\frac{\left(x+\frac{1}{2}\right)^{x+\frac{1}{2}}\sqrt{2}}% {e^{x+A(x,-\frac{1}{2})}}\left(\ln\left(x+\frac{1}{2}\right)+\frac{dA}{dx}% \right);
\psi(x+1)=\ln\left(x+\frac{1}{2}\right)+\frac{dA}{dx};
\psi(x+1)=\ln\left(x+\frac{1}{2}\right)+\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{\zeta(2n+1,x+% 1)}{(2n+1)2^{2n}} (5)

2.8 Polygamma Functions

Polygamma Functions are the logarithmic derivatives of the Gamma function, defined as follows:

\frac{d^{m+1}}{dx^{m+1}}\ln\Gamma(x)=\psi^{m}(x);
\frac{d\psi(x+1)}{dx}=\psi^{1}(x+1);
\psi^{1}(x+1)=\frac{1}{x+\frac{1}{2}}-\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{\zeta(2n+2,x+1)% }{2^{2n}};
\psi^{2}(x+1)=\frac{-1}{\left(x+\frac{1}{2}\right)^{2}}+\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}% \frac{(2n+2)\zeta(2n+3,x+1)}{2^{2n}};
\psi^{3}(x+1)=\frac{2}{\left(x+\frac{1}{2}\right)^{3}}-\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}% \frac{(2n+2)(2n+3)\zeta(2n+4,x+1)}{2^{2n}};

In general, based on differentiation rules, we have:

\psi^{y}(x+1)=(-1)^{y-1}\left(\frac{(y-1)!}{\left(x+\frac{1}{2}\right)^{y}}-% \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{(2n+y)!\,\zeta(2n+y+1,x+1)}{2^{2n}(2n+1)!}\right) (6)
\psi^{y}(x+1)=(-1)^{y-1}\left(\frac{(y-1)!}{\left(x+\frac{1}{2}\right)^{y}}-% \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{(2n+y)!\,\zeta(2n+y+1,x+1)}{2^{2n}(2n)(2n+1)(2n-1)!}\right)

Let:

f(n)=\frac{1}{2^{2n}(2n)(2n+1)} (7)

Then:

\psi^{y}(x+1)=(-1)^{y-1}\left(\frac{(y-1)!}{\left(x+\frac{1}{2}\right)^{y}}-% \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{f(n)(2n+y)!\,\zeta(2n+y+1,x+1)}{(2n-1)!}\right) (8)

3 Findings and Proofs

3.1 Theorem 1

If we define the functions K_{b}(n) and K_{e}(n) as follows for every n\in\mathbb{N}:

\displaystyle K_{b}(n) \displaystyle=\sum_{\lambda\vDash n}\frac{(-1)^{l(\lambda)}}{\prod_{i=1}^{l(% \lambda)}(2b_{i}+1)!},\quad n\geq 1 (9)
\displaystyle K_{e}(n) \displaystyle=\sum_{\lambda\vDash n}\frac{(-1)^{l(\lambda)}}{\prod_{i=1}^{l(% \lambda)}(2b_{i})!},\quad n\geq 1 (10)

where \lambda=(b_{1},b_{2},\ldots,b_{k}) represents the ordered partitions of n and l(\lambda)=k is the length of each partition, then the following relations hold for Bernoulli and Euler numbers:

\displaystyle B_{2n} \displaystyle=-\frac{(2n)!}{2^{2n}-2}\times K_{b}(n) (11)
\displaystyle E_{2n} \displaystyle=(2n)!\times K_{e}(n) (12)

In addition , the functions K_{b}(n) and K_{e}(n) can be computed recursively by the following relations:

K_{b}(n)=\begin{cases}1&n=0\\ -\sum_{n^{\prime}=0}^{n-1}\frac{K_{b}(n^{\prime})}{(2(n-n^{\prime})+1)!}&n\geq 1% \end{cases} (13)
K_{e}(n)=\begin{cases}1&n=0\\ -\sum_{n^{\prime}=0}^{n-1}\frac{K_{e}(n^{\prime})}{(2(n-n^{\prime}))!}&n\geq 1% \end{cases} (14)

1. The function h(m_{0}) can be computed as an infinite sum of the function p with the corresponding coefficients.

2. We denote the coefficient of each p(m_{z}) by T(m_{z}), which is formed by the product of several functions j(a,b) such that if we have:

3.8 Lemma 3

For every \text{Re}(x)>-\frac{1}{2}, the following relation holds for the Gamma function:

3.9 Proof

We begin with the following known relation for the Gamma function from [6]:

\Gamma(x+1)=\frac{\left(x+\frac{1}{2}\right)^{x+\frac{1}{2}}\sqrt{2\pi}}{e^{x+% \frac{1}{2}}}\times\exp\left(-\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{\zeta(2n,x+1)}{(2n+4n^{% 2})\,2^{2n}}\right) (1)

Let:

U=\sum_{m_{0}=1}^{\infty}\frac{\zeta\left(2m_{0},x+1\right)}{\left(2m_{0}+4m_{% 0}^{2}\right)\,2^{2m_{0}}}

Thus, we can write:

U=\sum_{m_{0}=1}^{\infty}f\left(m_{0}\right)\,h\left(m_{0}\right) (25)

As demonstrated in the previous section of the paper, the function h\left(m_{0}\right) can be expanded in terms of the functions p as follows:

h\left(m_{0}\right)=p\left(m_{0}\right)+\sum_{z=1}^{\infty}g\left(z+m_{0},m_{0% }\right)\,p\left(z+m_{0}\right) (19)

This expansion possesses the following properties:

  • For n=m_{0}, the coefficient of p(n) in the expansion of h\left(m_{0}\right) is equal to 1.

  • For n=m_{0}+c, where c\in\mathbb{N}, the coefficient of p(n) in the expansion of h\left(m_{0}\right) is equal to g\left(n,m_{0}\right).

In the following, we will use these properties of the expansion of h to extract the coefficients of p(n) from the function U. For this purpose, we first need to determine which h functions, upon expansion, yield p(n).

U=\sum_{m_{0}=1}^{\infty}f\left(m_{0}\right)\,h\left(m_{0}\right)=\sum_{m_{0}=% 1}^{n}f\left(m_{0}\right)\,h\left(m_{0}\right)+\sum_{m_{0}=n+1}^{\infty}f\left% (m_{0}\right)\,h\left(m_{0}\right)

According to equation (19), for m_{0}>n, the function p(n) does not appear in the expansion of h\left(m_{0}\right), therefore:

U=\sum_{m_{0}=1}^{n}f\left(m_{0}\right)\,h\left(m_{0}\right)+\text{(sum of % other terms without }p(n)\text{)}

Consequently, to find the coefficient of p(n), we only examine the first part:

U=\sum_{m_{0}=1}^{n}f\left(m_{0}\right)\,h\left(m_{0}\right)+\ldots
U=f(n)\,h(n)+\sum_{m_{0}=1}^{n-1}f\left(m_{0}\right)\,h\left(m_{0}\right)+\ldots

Based on the explanations provided earlier:

  1. 1.

    The coefficient of p(n) in the expansion of h(n) is equal to one. Therefore, one of the coefficients of p(n) in the function U will be f(n).

  2. 2.

    For every n>m_{0}, the coefficient of p(n) in the expansion of h\left(m_{0}\right) is equal to g\left(n,m_{0}\right). Thus, the other coefficients of p(n) in the function U, for every m_{0}\leq n-1, will be f\left(m_{0}\right)\,g\left(n,m_{0}\right).

Therefore, the sum of the coefficients of p(n) in the expansion of U is equal to:

a_{n}=f(n)+\sum_{m_{0}=1}^{n-1}g\left(n,m_{0}\right)\,f\left(m_{0}\right) (24)

Now, having the coefficients a_{n}, we can expand U in terms of the functions p(n) as follows:

U=\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}a_{n}\,p(n) (26)

Substituting equation (26) into equation (1) yields the following relation for the Gamma function:

\Gamma\left(x+\frac{1}{2}\right)=\left(\frac{x}{e}\right)^{x}\sqrt{2\pi}\,% \times\,\exp\left(-\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}a_{n}\,p(n)\right);

3.15 Connection with Euler Numbers

First, we define the function K_{e}(n) as the following determinant:

K_{e}(n)=\begin{vmatrix}1&0&0&\cdots&1\\ \frac{1}{2!}&1&0&\cdots&0\\ \frac{1}{4!}&\frac{1}{2!}&1&\cdots&0\\ \vdots&\vdots&\vdots&\ddots&\vdots\\ \frac{1}{(2n)!}&\frac{1}{(2n-2)!}&\frac{1}{(2n-4)!}&\cdots&\frac{1}{2!}&0\end{vmatrix} (34)

In article [4], the following relations for computing Bernoulli and Euler numbers based on determinants are provided:

B_{2n}=-\frac{(2n)!}{2^{2n}-2}\times\begin{vmatrix}1&0&0&\cdots&1\\ \frac{1}{3!}&1&0&\cdots&0\\ \frac{1}{5!}&\frac{1}{3!}&1&\cdots&0\\ \vdots&\vdots&\vdots&\ddots&\vdots\\ \frac{1}{(2n+1)!}&\frac{1}{(2n-1)!}&\frac{1}{(2n-3)!}&\cdots&\frac{1}{3!}&0% \end{vmatrix}
E_{2n}=(2n)!\times\begin{vmatrix}1&0&0&\cdots&1\\ \frac{1}{2!}&1&0&\cdots&0\\ \frac{1}{4!}&\frac{1}{2!}&1&\cdots&0\\ \vdots&\vdots&\vdots&\ddots&\vdots\\ \frac{1}{(2n)!}&\frac{1}{(2n-2)!}&\frac{1}{(2n-4)!}&\cdots&\frac{1}{2!}&0\end{vmatrix}

Therefore, according to the definition of K_{e}(n) in equation (34), the Euler relation can be rewritten as follows:

E_{2n}=(2n)!\times K_{e}(n) (35)

Also, substituting equation (33) into the formula for Bernoulli numbers, the determinant representation of K_{b}(n) is obtained as follows:

K_{b}(n)=\begin{vmatrix}1&0&0&\cdots&1\\ \frac{1}{3!}&1&0&\cdots&0\\ \frac{1}{5!}&\frac{1}{3!}&1&\cdots&0\\ \vdots&\vdots&\vdots&\ddots&\vdots\\ \frac{1}{(2n+1)!}&\frac{1}{(2n-1)!}&\frac{1}{(2n-3)!}&\cdots&\frac{1}{3!}&0% \end{vmatrix}

Comparing the two determinants K_{b}(n) and K_{e}(n) shows that they share the same structure, the only difference being that the odd factorials (from 3! to (2n+1)!) in K_{b}(n) are replaced by even factorials (from 2! to (2n)!) in K_{e}(n).

Consequently, if the function F is defined as follows:

F((2i+1)!)=K_{b}(n)\quad\quad n\geq i

Then:

F\left((2i)!\right)=K_{e}(n)\quad\quad n\geq i

Therefore, considering relations (9) and (13) previously obtained for K_{b}(n), and by applying the modifications related to replacing odd factorials with even factorials, the equivalent relations for K_{e}(n) can be deduced as follows:

K_{e}(n)=-\sum_{n^{\prime}=0}^{n-1}\frac{K_{e}(n^{\prime})}{(2(n-n^{\prime}))!% }\quad\quad n\geq 1 (36)
K_{e}(n)=\sum_{\lambda\vDash n}\frac{(-1)^{l(\lambda)}}{\prod_{i=1}^{l(\lambda% )}(2b_{i})!}\quad\quad n\geq 1 (37)

These relations provide independent computational methods for K_{e}(n) that can be used for the efficient computation of Euler numbers.

4 Conclusion

In this paper, we have established a unified framework that connects Bernoulli numbers and Euler numbers to ordered partitions of integers through the functions K_{b}(n) and K_{e}(n). By deriving recursive relations and combinatorial interpretations for these functions, we have developed novel methods for computing these important sequences, which originate from expansions of special functions such as the zeta, Gamma, and polygamma functions.

This connection not only reveals a deeper interplay between number theory and combinatorics, but also offers practical advantages for numerical evaluations. Representing these numbers through ordered partitions provides a better understanding of their structure and enables more efficient computations. This approach may pave the way for future research in generalizing these relationships to other numerical sequences and their applications in analytic and combinatorial number theory.
